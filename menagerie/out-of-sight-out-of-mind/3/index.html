There's no place like home
( Page 3 of 3 ) : Out of Sight, Out of Mind, by Amanda Hughes

post a comment.

So, what do you think: psychics or psychotics?

Give us your Opinion!

Anders advertised herself as a love advisor, and found that the job did not require psychic ability of any sort. Most of her clients already knew their own hearts, and sought Anders’ help to affirm the soundness of their decisions. “Most of them would pretty much just tell me what they were looking for as far as advice is concerned,” she says. “I’d tell them what they wanted to hear or make the choices they obviously weren’t confident enough to make for themselves. Sometimes, even if it was bad news, all they wanted was confirmation.”

Most people who called Anders for psychic counseling had minor problems, but she remembers one client who, entangled in an increasingly desperate situation, reached out to her for help.

“I talked to a woman with a deadbeat boyfriend,” Anders says. “A serious bad news guy. We talked for a long time, and it was heartbreaking. She needed a friend, someone to tell her this guy was a loser and bad for her, and just abusive. I sat there talking to her – I didn’t know her – and I wanted to grab every person in her life and scream at them. Why weren’t they helping her dump this guy?”

After their session was over, Anders never heard from her again. She left the job shortly afterward. To this day, she doesn’t know what happened to the woman. Anders no longer believes in the existence of psychic abilities.

But what if Anders’ advice swayed her, and the woman was able to leave her abusive partner? What possible harm could come from seeking psychic counsel?

“Well, the most immediate and tangible negative effect is on the person’s wallet,” Lancaster says. “No matter how much or how little a person pays for a psychic reading, they receive literally nothing for their money but a few minutes of bad theater, and perhaps an object lesson on spending their money more wisely.”

But psychic readings have emotional side effects as well. A session with a psychic may provide someone with temporary relief from the anxiety of a stressful job or family life or, as with many of Anders’s clients, it may spur an indecisive person into action. In these instances, the emotional ramifications may not be so serious. But in cases like Anders’ abused client, a psychic’s advice may interfere with natural emotional processes. With self-esteem damaged from an abusive relationship, the woman may find she is unable to make decisions for herself, a pattern that is only reinforced in her session with a psychic. Additionally, if the woman acts on the basis psychic advice and leaves the relationship without having prepared herself emotionally, she may find herself drawn back in later, with disastrous results.

These sensitive psychological matters are best left to qualified mental health professionals, according to Lancaster. “I have occasionally received e-mails on the site from people asking me, ‘If Sylvia Browne is a fraud, how can I find out about my deceased loved one?’ I usually tell these people to look into seeing a grief counselor,” Lancaster says. “A strong case can be made that what psychics do actually interferes with, and even halts the natural progression of the grieving process.”

When it comes to families seeking psychic counsel to recover a lost loved one, the stakes are even higher. According to Lancaster, psychic advisors cost police departments precious time and resources during the course of a missing persons investigation when they follow up on tips from families’ psychic consultants. Unlike other, anonymous tips detectives might pursue with varying results, checking up on psychic predictions always amounts to chasing meaningless guesses.

Families of missing and dead relatives are in a uniquely vulnerable position—desperate for answers that, of course, no one can give them.

Sylvia Browne constructed a reputation for herself over the years as a successful psychic advisor in missing persons cases. She’s done this largely through appearances on the now-defunct Montel Williams Show, where she appeared with families of lost loved ones, often abducted children, and made general predictions about the abductee’s whereabouts. When they originally aired, these episodes of The Montel Williams Show were among the program’s highest-rated. During each program, Browne gave families predictions with just enough detail to maintain the illusion of specificity (the kidnapper’s sex and skin color, the terrain where the child’s body will be found), while sticking to high-probability guesses (The body will be found near water), or nearly impossible-to-prove predictions (The child is now a sex worker in a foreign country).

Browne claims her missing persons predictions have an eight-five-percent accuracy rate, but the results of a three-year investigation published earlier this year demonstrated that “Browne has not even been mostly correct in a single case.”

Families of missing and dead relatives, however, are not evaluating a psychic’s performance based on statistics, probability, or success rates. They are in a uniquely vulnerable position—desperate for answers that, of course, no one can give them. Just as the missing loved one is victimized by his or her abductor, Lancaster posits, so the families are victimized by their psychic advisors, a “second wave of predators” who descend on the grieved when they are at their lowest and most credulous. The only thing these families can be faulted for is hope—hope that their child can be restored to them, hope that someone can help relay a message of love to beyond the grave.

This mention of vulnerability brings me to my explanation of why this article has focused on the evidence against supernatural ability, and why I have not given voice to the opposition—the psychics themselves. Simply put, there is no other side of the argument against psychic ability, because it is not an argument. There is a preponderance of evidence to the contrary, a mainstream scientific consensus that no such abilities exist, and a scores of communication tricks like cold reading that consistently deliver supernatural-like results with perfectly natural explanations. I posit that presenting the psychic perspective in this article would only give yet another platform to an industry composed of individuals who are at best profoundly self-deluded and at worst predatory.

I considered consulting a psychic or medium myself as part my preparations for this article. In the interest of full disclosure I will say that my beloved maternal grandmother died during my second week of research. I do not need, I hope, to spell out the excruciating circumstance I found myself in. I was presented with a unique opportunity and, I confess, I was afraid. I was afraid to put myself in a room with a psychic who might read the pain off my face and tease the fact of my grandmother’s death out of me. I was afraid this stranger would discuss her with me, guess how I felt about her, and take a stab at how she felt about me.

I was afraid not because I would believe the psychic’s words were true—there is little chance of that. I hope I would not let my own grief distort my perspective. No, I was afraid to pay someone money to lie to me. I was afraid to allow someone to put words in my dead grandmother’s mouth, and color my living memories of her with a stranger’s best guesses.

I wish so fervently that supernatural abilities did exist. I would pay any psychic consultant’s fee to speak with my grandmother again. It is unbearably painful to live with the questions: Did she know that I still loved her? Did she still love me? Did she resent me for being halfway across the country when she died? It is so, so hard to live in the gray area, to accept that no one can ever answer these questions for me. But I’m beginning to find ways to live with the pain. It is an achingly beautiful fantasy, to believe that someone could predict our happy futures and convey our messages to lost loved ones. But it is a fantasy.
