There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Trouble in Paradise: the Principality of Podmajersky, by Emily Bernhard

At the encouragement of their realtor, the gallery spaces throughout Podland – both the large galleries and the tiny workshops in which the artists also live – flaunt their wares on the second Friday evening of every month, opening their doors and serving free wine and cheese to neighbors and pilgrims alike. This ongoing event is an important harbinger of Pod’s progress, as hip, artsy types invade the neighborhood and are free to give themselves a private tour of these enchanting spaces while they crawl the galleries. In fact, it was on a Second Friday that I first bore witness to these interiors: ceilings that would tickle the ears of a giraffe; airy, open studios replete with vintage wooden floors and skylights; spacious lofts, laden with tucked-away beds and kitchen supplies, floating quaintly above the workspaces; a gloriously unobstructed rooftop view of the skyline—and all this at rates only slightly inflated.

A Podmajersky information office, only open on Second Fridays, meanwhile sets up shop in a space on the corner of Eighteenth and Halsted. Along the top of one of its large glass windows, floating gray letters announce that the visitor has entered the “Chicago Arts District.” According to their website, Podmajersky “founded the Chicago Arts District to assist with strategic development” and “provide overall support” to the artists and businesses in the neighborhood, though for their purposes the neighborhood is confined to the district where they hold property (An aerial view on the same website reveals that the district in question encompasses about sixteen square blocks, an area bordered at the north and south by Eighteenth street and Canalport and east and west by Sangamom and Ruble). Yet marketing for the Chicago Arts District excludes an abundant array of galleries that are geographically and culturally relevant to Pilsen, as they lie, of course, just beyond the jurisdiction of the Principality of Podmajersky. They have done such an excellent job of suggesting that a cultural, historical, or governmental authority once bestowed this title on the area that the term has entered a ubiquitous usage. Nevertheless, while prospective tenants and business-owners may be more receptive to Podmajersky’s mission when it is put forth by this “burgeoning, grass-roots organization,” the duplicitous strategy seems to have been ineffective in retaining artists and galleries for any long period of time. Moreover, although efforts to bring visitors to the neighborhood often draw on the perceived edginess of the bohemian and financially apathetic residents, the slow influx of a mixed-hipster crowd coincides with rent increases.

While Pod the Elder identifies himself as a crusader, a cowboy, and a hero to the community vision in tow, John Podmajersky III – who now curates the streets of the Chicago Arts District – has gutted and rehabilitated Pod II’s decades-old, purported “vision” of providing a stimulating community to his artist-tenants. John III has refocused efforts on filling the long-empty, street-level storefronts running along Halsted Street, between Eighteenth and Canalport. Turnover is remarkably high for residents and renters, with even long-time gallery-owners moving onwards. The exodus notably includes Robin Monique Rios and her 4Art, Inc. gallery; Dubhe Carreño; and Moka Gallery. John III has pursued a vision of a new breed of artist: the “creative entrepreneur.”

Podmajersky has recently disseminated a series of double-sided signs that project flag-like from the empty storefront spaces along Halsted, between Eighteenth and Nineteenth streets. The north-facing side of each sign asks you to “imagine…,” while the south-facing side proposes a hypothetical business for each embarrassingly undesired space. They premeditate the existence of, for example, a boutique, framing studio, design studio, and showroom. Although the suggestion is innocent enough, it contributes to the image of a “creative entrepreneur” who, ideally, purchases ready-made designs for his business cards and replicates a spread in the CB2 catalog for his home office.

Perhaps the word-pairing “creative entrepreneur” is as empty as the spaces themselves. Who is this artist who chooses to be an artist in the interest of establishing a cottage industry of financial gain? What is the modern sociopolitical force at play that seeks to merge our creative efforts into a business model? With the suggestion that they want to help artists in helping themselves and in escaping dependence on state-funding and charity, the Podmajersky brand denies the value of patronage; it also suggests that an artist need not work non-artistic jobs to compensate for the dangerous undertaking of producing a masterpiece. The underlying message: “If you take art as seriously as we do, you’ll make art that sells.” As it appears, the mission of the “art people” is to “create” but mainly to “live” and to “work,” constantly—that is, to open their homes on a monthly basis, entice the dreaming masses to join their community, and market the trinkets that pay their rent and consume all their time.

The risks undertaken by the avant-garde artist should not, of course, be confused with those taken by a speculator, as they are here. So who, then, is the “creative entrepreneur” that John III seeks? Would it really be so shocking to refer to him simply as an entrepreneur? Yes, it would—because that label would show that the ideal tenants’ contribution to the arts is exactly the same as Podmajersky’s.
