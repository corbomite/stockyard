There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Painting Us out of a Corner, by Lily Ye
Art/Photography Credit: Taylor Burton

Most of the visitors to HallMfg are students, or at least they look like students—young, bending under heavy backpacks, slurping up Starbucks. Most people have not heard of the gallery and, if caught looking in the window, must be beckoned inside. If they can be persuaded to enter, Freddy shows them the workspace and tells them about the chairs. Visitors typically spend five to ten minutes there before leaving, at which point, I am told with great emphasis, Freddy prompts them to sign the guestbook and tell their friends to swing by. (There were no other visitors to the gallery during my half-hour stay.)

All of this Freddy revealed to me with confidence, but he became extremely unsure when I began to ask about the Pop-Up Art Loop initiative. He had been working at the HallMfg gallery for less than a week, and he had only moved to Chicago a month and a half earlier – from the small town of Princeville, Illinois – because his wife had just started grad school in the city. Freddy had heard about the job from his brother-in-law and insisted on riding only the Red Line to work so he “won’t get lost.” As I left, he shouted out to me, cheerily, “Have your friends visit!”

The two other galleries I visited last Wednesday afternoon were simply window displays, one featuring paintings and sculpture, and one showcasing a television playing “underground” films. Had I not been searching for them, in spite of their distinctive green logos, these galleries would have been lost in the ads that surround them. These ads span the full length of the windows, while the galleries have but small slivers of glass open for viewing. Standing at the corner of each street, I saw construction workers, couples, people in smart suits all walk by, oblivious. Only the occasional pedestrian, without a phone or companion, might turn his head to give a passing glance.

Searching for the chartreuse beacon of the Chicago Photography Collective’s gallery, I crossed the street between Madison and Wabash four times, once being misled by the nearby Cortiva Institute’s use of the same color. I found the gallery nestled obscurely between a TCF Bank and a 7-11.

Inside, Ron Gordon and Sandra Steinbrecher were preparing for a show scheduled for the following Friday. Hammer in one hand and electric drill in the other, Ron, who has been a photographer for forty years, eagerly recounted the brief history of the Chicago Photography Collective—starting as group of four friends, it had quickly expanded into a network of twenty photographers dedicated to as many styles. Sandra, who was once a student of Ron’s, expressed equal enthusiasm for the Collective, saying that it enables photographers to share their work and support each other. Getting this group together and starting the pop-up gallery took only a month.

Pop-Up Art Loop wants to move just as swiftly: Ty Tabing, its executive director, hopes that between fifteen and twenty galleries will be active by April. The content of these galleries remains unplanned. While the galleries’ first artists were personal contacts of the members of the initiative, a curatorial committee has now developed to evaluate proposals from a broader community of artists. (These proposals must still meet the approval of each building’s landlord.)

Nelita, who supervises the Chicago Photography Collective gallery, told me that a group of six women, all photographers, came in earlier this month after they had read about pop-up galleries in a newspaper. “They wanted to start a similar movement in their hometown,” she recalled. Both HallMfg and the Chicago Photography Collective prominently displayed the same Time Out Chicago article examining a past exhibit of the initiative’s and its methods and aims in general.

Like Nelita, everyone else with whom I spoke viewed the pop-up-gallery phenomenon as a situation in which everyone wins. The landlord’s space is “dolled up” and open for potential leasers to view, artists have a new venue to display their art, and the city is made more beautiful because it is made more “organic.” The visitors in the Chicago Photography Collective gallery, waved in by Nelita, all reacted as if they had been let in on a secret or received a special treat.

For now, these galleries don’t have the flamboyance or insistence that their name imparts; unassuming and unobtrusive, they do not “pop up” at you like a jack-in-the-box. Tabing acknowledges that they have not yet reached the “critical mass” necessary to begin having a notable effect on the city, and the green that marks the galleries seems true to their nature: they are young and slowly sprouting, widening the cracks between the expected and the desirable at an imperceptible pace.

If they can make you look once, however, they do make you look twice—and in this city, that’s an art. There’s now at least a little less painting by the numbers.

You can learn more about the Pop-Up Art Loop Alliance at popupartloop.com.
