A Piece of Crap

At first I didn’t know how to react. There was no stage, no audience. I initially mistook the whole thing as vulgarity but soon felt embarrassed when I realized that it was art-for-art’s-sake.

by Jeremy Cox | 23 Jun. 2009
Tawdry Details:
2 comments »
Volume I, Issue 1

What happened to good, old-fashioned skill?” This is, of course, the American majority’s usual reply to the exponents of art-for-art’s-sake. A blank canvas slapped with a nonsensical title, a can of soup spilt decorously across a tablecloth, a bouquet of daisies arranged in a crystal vase filled to the brim with frothy, dehydrated urine: Such works “can’t” be art because, against the better taste of most Americans, they forsake the idea that art should be symbolic, well-executed, and beautiful. These envelope-pushers address the bourgeois establishment with the contention that the existence of art hinges on just one characteristic: an artist’s defining it as such.

Like most Americans, I labeled this brand of art as self-important, self-indulgent hogwash; contrary to most critics and museums, I accused this genre of vandalizing a rich artistic tradition. Only recently did I grasp how wrong I was. Chancing upon what I’d venture to call the “Mona Lisa” of postmodernism, I realized that it takes extraordinary bravado and integrity to snub the role of audience, bite one’s thumb at the bourgeois establishment, and produce art that would – in no sane or sober circumstance – sell.

Meandering the side streets of Michigan Avenue in order to delay arriving at my girlfriend’s apartment, I stumbled upon this piece, a performance piece, when it was already underway. At first I didn’t know how to react. There was no stage, no audience. I initially mistook the whole thing as vulgarity but soon felt embarrassed when I realized that it was art-for-art’s-sake. The artist wasn’t concerned with making money or cultivating a following, mainstream or underground. Instead, he was exercising his right to create on his own terms and had taken care to remove the performance as far out of sight as he could.

Picture the scene. A dark alley. A performer costumed in the rags of a transient. Wild, unwashed hair and a beard matted with the appetizing contents of two trash cans and twice as many 40s. The scene had all the trappings of a perfectionist at work.

Curtain up, and the performer rose. Off his strategically placed newspaper mattress, he struggled along a brick wall towards the cramped space behind a dumpster. At this point, he dropped his trousers and squatted in the shadows. A squelching sound emerged like a warning shot, a warning shot aimed at the bourgeois establishment’s stranglehold on artistic expression. Within seconds, blasts shook the alleyway. The performer began to wail like a banshee; and a tear formed in his eye, mid-push. This moving performance brought a tear too my eye, too. Real catharsis.

He clawed at his pants and heaved them waist-wards without a moment to air-dry. At such a brave conclusion, I could not but applaud. As I suddenly realized, here was an ingenious commentary on the place of experimental art in our society. What a bold gesture in the face of the Academy!

Upon hearing my applause, the artist turned to me and, without breaking character, asked, “What the fuck do you want?”
I was not sure whether I was supposed to answer and allow the piece to become participatory or simply continue watching; indeed, I wondered if I were not unlike the unseasoned symphony-goer who claps between movements. Yet all was well as the performer drew closer and repeated his inquiry, indicating that he was still sticking to his script. “What the fuck do you want?”
I was dumbstruck, I was starstruck; and, despite all the questions a critic would die to ask of this visionary, all I could manage was: “What do you call it?”

He answered. “I call it a pile of shit, you fucking pervert. How about I—”

Some philistines from the local precinct suddenly grabbed him by the arms and whisked him away to their patrol car. While I was disappointed that my time with the artist was cut short, I feel that the police unwittingly spoke to the authoritative roles of tradition and taste in art-making, to the artificial barriers these roles impose on creative output.

The performance was a brilliant one; and, if you get a chance to see a reprise (though I doubt an artist so impervious to the hackneyed effects of traditionalism would humor such an asinine desire), I recommend it. At the very least, the piece took guts.
