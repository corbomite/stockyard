There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 5 ) : Hello, Daniel, by Paula Carter
Art/Photography Credit: Licensed under Creative Commons

I decide to bring a chair to the kitchen window and sit and watch the high-rise that is also a convalescence home and is run by the Little Sisters of the Poor. It is behind my house, across a parking lot; and, because it is a ways away, I can see most of the hundred windows at once. Each window has white curtains. I can sometimes see the outlines of the patients on the second floor. The angle is wrong everywhere else. But I can always see the sisters, who are standing up and who wear white habits and come into the rooms and purposefully open or close the tiny white curtains, like an advent calendar. Yet today there are only a few curtains open, and none at all on the second floor.

While I am watching, the daughter from the neighbors on the left comes out into the yard. It is early spring. Too cold to go without a coat, and yet she is wearing only a long pink sweater that is cut in a V at the neck, revealing her blue-white skin. These neighbors have lived there for a long time: a Greek family who rent out the basement, second, and third floors to uncles, sisters, and cousins. The first floor is always the same husband and wife with their two daughters. Sometimes on summer evenings I can hear the couple fight. I don’t know what they’re saying because they fight in Greek. I make up the conversations. I think they fight because he is gone too much, leaving in a black car that idles by the curb on Tuesdays and returns on Fridays. But they could be about the wife, who flirts with whoever is living in the basement apartment now, a man always dressed in brown—in brown T-shirts, pants, and shoes.

The girl has her mother’s hair, and although she is still young, she doesn’t have any simple beauty. Rather, she is awkward: arms sticking out a little to the sides, dangling there helplessly beside her breasts. I have seen her walk up to the corner to catch the 88 bus, wearing florescent colored tights, wearing heels that make her appear younger rather than older. Her little sister, who must be about eleven, is all liquid with energy, olive-skinned, and will probably be a beaut.

The girl stands in the backyard and looks up through the power lines to the sky. I wonder if she, too, is worried about the power lines—if she, too, has heard the warning beeps. I remember when one of the power lines out front came crashing down and hit the pavement throwing sparks until it grew up into a curved neck. It was able to find its way into the house, between the window and the sill. I sat down on the mattress and watched it push its way through, splitting again and again in order to fit. Pushing one black thread through at a time. I waited, realizing it was best not to run.

(Now that I think about it, however, that must not have happened at all. I’m not sure why I remember it.)

The girl sits down on the back step, which I imagine is cold. She takes out a cigarette from a pocket in her skirt and smokes it. She fingers the edge of her skirt, where it meets her calf. I see that she is looking towards the Little Sisters of the Poor. Then she looks down at her hands, which I can’t see well from where I am, then she stands to go inside, leaving the butt of the cigarette in an old coffee can that the man in brown, who is living in the basement apartment, uses when he smokes.

Looking out the back window, I fall asleep in my chair and awake when the phone rings. The shell-less egg has hatched into a million small lizards, which have crawled out of the bowl and are swarming on the table. They move in bursts. I try to focus on one, but it moves when I set my eyes on it, like very dim light in the corner of the eye.

The phone rings again. I look at it on the wall, then peer into the bowl on the table. The delicate mass sits there, still. The color is a bright orange now, the membrane stretched out and transparent. It just sits there. Still. But sometimes, even right now, in moments that are not even moments but infinitesimal spaces that I can not see or measure, in these moments the membrane is breaking, pulling apart, and soon the egg will split.

I know it is my father on the phone. The answering machine answers after some time.

“Daniel,” he says. “Daniel … Daniel.” He asks if I am home.

He tells me he will be over around seven.

He asks me where I want to go for dinner.

He suggests Harold’s, which is where we always go.
