There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Eternal Returns, by Neil Root

“That’s a shilling, please.”

“But I won’t be here much longer.”

“That doesn’t matter. You pay for the time you’ve spent.”

He digs into his pocket and surrenders several pound notes. The coins he can use; the coins have weight.

Out of the deckchair, he’s off the pebbles – off the millions of pebbles – in a matter of minutes; his toes touch sand. His big feet leave deep imprints, but in a few hours they’ll be gone; in a few hours, his footsteps will follow him.

3. Overhaul

Here and not and here again, a streetlight blinks with struggling power. The clock in the square chimes midnight, but the time is eight minutes to the hour. Gray streets run only to where his eye observes.

Arnold is strolling back from his publisher’s. His first novel took almost nine months to write; and, like the labor, the birth wasn’t easy. Now, after an endless stream of rejection letters, he’s finally made the delivery; he’s made the sale, and his novel will hit the shelves of bookstores everywhere by Christmas. Fickle, yes; but life has its way of coming around.

On the corner, the roll-down gate at the newsstand clatters to the pavement; there, a naked bulb sputters out. Several men scuttle up the opposite side of the street. Their overcoats flying through the wind like phantoms, they dash with brown parcels through the wind and into the Underground, where the jazzy magazines under their arms will offer them a brief consolation. Arnold can’t relate to them: His consolation waits at home.

Home isn’t far from here. He turns into a residential street off the square, the streetlight blinking far behind him.
A lantern glimmers in his window. Marie will be looking forwards to seeing him. He can’t wait to tell her the news. She hasn’t been herself lately, and this should cheer her up.

He walks up the path to their first home, a rented room—or an “artist’s studio,” as Arnold likes to call it. In the moonlight, the few cars on their street gleam with arrogance: Their owners are very proud of them.

He pushes the key into the door; he turns it; he pulls it out. Wiping his feet, he decides to kick off his shoes: They’ve just bought a new carpet, and Marie likes to keep up appearances. Then, as he turns to snuff out the lantern—then he sees her.
It’s the first time he’s seen her naked without becoming aroused.

Yet her body looks gray, her face almost blue, the cord cutting into her neck no less tight. Marie shoots him a sidelong glance.
He sits down on the wooden chair, and Marie still dangles above him. The expression on her face holds a final, teasing smile. Arnold questions if she ever smiled at him like that, wonders if Tommy would smile back now. He springs up and begins to punch her stomach as a boxer might pound a frozen slab of meat. Exhausted, he collapses into the chair again.

After a few minutes, he stands up and slips back into his shoes. He walks behind Marie and kisses her on the bottom. She’s cold to the taste. He snuffs out the lantern and strolls through the front door, leaving her exactly as he left her that morning, leaving her exactly as he left her the morning before.
