There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 3 ) : The Settlement, by Nisha Coleman

“How’s the stained glass going?” he asks once the song is over.

“It’s great,” she says turning down the volume. “I just sold a big piece last week, an overhead lamp.” She is being honest, she assures herself, not boastful.

“Is that your only source of income?”

“Yes it is,” she replies and waits for him to comment on her ability to earn a salary. Her defence is crouched and waiting between her teeth, but he merely sighs.

“So when’s the wedding?” she asks after a few minutes of silence.

“Oh, it’ll be this summer.”

“You haven’t set a date yet?”

“Well, I had to take care of our divorce,” His voice has a thin patronizing edge.

“Right, so are the kids going?”

“Of course.”

“You’ve told them?”

“Not about the wedding. They know about Julie. They’ve met and they get along wonderfully.”

She’s merely seeing how much he knows. The kids have already told her what they think of Julie.

“They’re not in love,” had said their daughter, grimacing as if referring to an STD. “She’s only a few years older than me.”

“He struts around with her like she’s a prize,” added their younger son in disgust.

They had grown distant with their father over the last few years, had started seeing his faults, slowly at first, like spotting the first stars of the night. Before long, the sky is saturated.

Her son had inherited his father’s features and is the same age now as when they had first met. She often catches herself staring at him, marvelling at their resemblance, the soft dark eyes, the broad shoulders, the slight chin crease. Sometimes when he walks into the room she gets shivers up her arms, like seeing a ghost.

“And you?” he asks.

“Me?”

“Are you seeing anybody?” The question is like a poke in the ribs.

“No,” she answers with controlled neutrality. “Not anymore,” she adds. “I mean, I’ve been with people, but I’m not with anyone at the moment.” She doesn’t want him to think she is still in love with him or upset about the affair. The affair was the best thing that ever happened to her. In many ways.

The rain picks up and begins to roar like static. It is a desperate, steady rain, violent, like thousands of tiny fists. She turns up the radio to drown it out.

“Good song, eh?” he says with a nod.

She hadn’t noticed. It’s Bruce Springsteen, Tunnel of Love. He joins in for the chorus, singing loudly and a semitone off key.

“Ever think,” he says when the song ends, “that maybe we were the right people at the wrong time?”

The thought taints the air like a sudden bad smell. “No,” she says after a brief pause. “I hadn’t thought about it.”

“I mean, it’s possible, right? We were so in love, weren’t we?”

This doesn’t makes sense. He is the one getting married. She spent years learning how to be alone and isn’t about to get dragged back to whatever he has in mind.

“God, remember that night in Banff?” he goes on. “In that flimsy old tent.” His gaze is focused straight ahead. “That storm! Something else, wasn’t it? Man, that was an incredible night.” He smiles and shakes his head wistfully.

She has no protection against this, is unable to hold off the oncoming flood of images, and is sent thrashing into old memories, ones sealed away for good reason.

They had been camping in unfamiliar mountains when a violent thunderstorm plucked them from slumber. They grabbed for each other in the darkness, at first in panic at the sudden thunder crack that rocked in their chests, but the fear quickly transformed into desire. They made love with the wind ripping through the trees, threatening to split the tent. Debilitating pleasure rose and shot with throbbing ripples throughout her body and with it came the realization that she could not be more in love, that she had reached her peak, her love threshold. Anything that came after would disappoint. She wept with bitter euphoria.

It took a few years, but their love did disintegrate. Eventually it left them completely, followed shortly after by the affair. The loss of love was most disturbing. It meant that anything, no matter how sacred, could be lost.

The rain pummels down with heavy sheets of grey. She turns on her hazard lights and slows. Most of the other cars have pulled off to the side and before long she does the same. They sit in silence as their breath obscures the windows and the rain smashes against the car. She closes her eyes and listens to its frustrated rhythms.
