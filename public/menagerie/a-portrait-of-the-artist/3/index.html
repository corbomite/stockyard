There's no place like home
( Page 3 of 3 ) : A Portrait of the Artist, by Mikayla Lynch

M: Why do you not paint eyes?

F: Because, as Modigliani says, when an artist paints the eyes, he gives personality to that icon or that figure. Once you paint the eye, you are painting the whole feeling of that person. I know it’s a cliché, but eyes really are the windows to the soul. So if you’re painting an icon, you do not want that sacred and secret part exposed. Weaknesses are shown through the eyes.

M: Do you see yourself always painting? Where do you see yourself in 10 years?—owning your own gallery, perhaps?

F: This is a funny question, to tell you the truth. I have this philosophy; let me explain it to you. I have visions, and I know what I want, but I don’t limit my future to what I want; I leave it open to see what there is. At this point right now, for example, there is something that I want but that I won’t mention—shouldn’t mention. In ten years, however, I do see myself painting – I see myself painting always – painting maybe in Europe. A quieter lifestyle, settled down: I’d like that. I’m approaching that lifestyle right now, I think. Besides being settled and expressing new ideas, there is a second part to my future vision—I’m doing well in the business right now, and that’s not all well for an artist; I need to think about what I am going to give to the world.

M: Your series of painted Buddhas is very popular; your exhibit at the Victor Hotel did well. Will you continue to work on them?

F: Let me tell you something about the Buddhas. I liked the series, but I stopped it for a while. I’m going to give it a little break because I don’t think it’s a subject that should be exploited; it’s very sacred. I plan to develop it slowly, put it aside until I’m comfortable with bringing it out again. It is something I will always be painting because I like the philosophy of Buddhism. But, right now, I am attempting something different: something that has to do with the human being, human wants, what humans think that they want, what humans go through and their connection to the world. The secret meanings, the symbolism, what we think we see but we don’t—that’s what I’m working on.

M: What inspired the switch from god to man?

F: This question makes me laugh all the time. I paint what I am living at the moment, what I have already lived. But, in times of desperation, in times when an opportunity fails to arise or I feel I have nothing to give, I choose to paint what I want to live. To me, paintings are like prayers. You paint because to make all your thoughts evaporate—you want to purge them, store them on the canvas. What you think is what you attract, so, therefore, I paint what I want to go through. I have to be really careful when I’m painting because it’s like a mantra. One time I painted a box and everybody told me it looked like coffin. The day that I finished, my friend called me and told me his mother had passed away that very morning. And my grandmother passed away soon afterwards.

M: So art, you would say, is your religion?

F: Yes, in a way it is.

M: Is the god of your religion you or is it someone like Salvador Dali?

F: Wow, I like that one; but now we’ll have to get deeper.

If I say that I do something religiously, then I imply that I perform a certain procedure; and religion, to me, is a philosophy, a belief system, that has procedures. You can be spiritual, or you can be religious, or you can be both. So I would say that my religion is my artwork and that my beliefs are very attached to what I do. My beliefs are diverse; I’m quite universal. I respect all religions and believe that each one touches some aspect of the Truth; it’s like the saying, “All roads lead to Rome.” Philosophy is my religion; artwork is my drive, my procedure to reach the higher power.

M: As we leave, Fidel, I should ask what impression you’d like to leave on the world. When people say, “Fidel Rodriguez,” what should they think about?

F: Art, in its way, transforms reality; but it does not transform people of its own power. That power, a four-letter word that I hope you’ll choose to print, is love. That’s the message I’d like to give.
