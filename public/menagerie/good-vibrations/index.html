Good Vibrations

The “massager” was lauded by women’s magazines and one of its early retailers, Sears Roebuck, as the quintessential gift for a woman, designed to preserve her youth and keep her neurotic outbursts at bay. 

by Magdalena Serafin | 31 Jul. 2010
Tawdry Details:
0 comments »
Volume II, Issue 2

The midday rays of the relentless July sun pour through the large display windows, only to be tempered by the crisp and pleasant commercial chill of man’s last defense against Chicago’s infamous summer. The haltered beams alight on the grid in their path: tidy little rows and columns of jars brimming with a rainbow’s spectrum of offerings. The clear containers lining the south wall boast an impressive array of all sorts, shapes, sizes, brands, colors, flavors: chocolate, strawberry, cherry, lemon—and sheepskin. This is certainly not an arrangement available at the local supermarket or confectioner’s, though some of the wares are very much edible.

Upon first heading east and away from the groaning rust of the Red Line, a casual visitor to the understated cityscape of Berwyn will venture towards Sheridan, a quiet, almost-suburban looking avenue that marks the border between the city proper and the lakefront area, save for the concrete raceway of Lake Shore Drive. After turning south and deeper into the folds of the picturesque neighborhood, she will pass the Christian Science Reading Room, a Seattle Sutton’s Healthy Eating establishment, and, with is name written in an innocently similar neon, Early to Bed. 

The common element among these toys is [...] that they’re all about as threatening and aggressive as a Sears catalog touting Tonka trucks.

If the visitor gives the place just a passing glance, she may think that Early to Bed is some variety of linen or bedding store rather than one of Chicago’s myriad sex shops.  Opposite the candy-bright array of condoms and lubricant, and set against the plush background of soft purples and greens, the store’s main draws twist, wind, wiggle, and protrude their way around the large expanse of wall space nearest the door, offset here and there by velveteen storage accessories and the occasional accent of a gold star or oversized feather. Some objects are squat, some are thin, some are curvy, and some show the face of a woodland creature or comically adorable centipede. The common element among these toys is, unlike the chains and leather and donut-mouthed blow-up dolls usually associated with sex shops, that they’re all about as threatening and aggressive as a Sears catalog touting Tonka trucks.

Then again, about a century ago, this was very much the standard operating procedure.

A bevy of artifacts as old as the Mediterranean world attest to the fact that non-organic phalluses have always been a common means of self-pleasure: urns and statuettes showing as much could well be described as the forerunners of pornography. Greater variety was introduced openly during the Italian Renaissance and then as part of a taboo counterculture in Victorian England. Yet the inception of the most buzz-worthy gadget can be traced back to America during the Industrial Revolution, when a physician named George Taylor designed a model that was, appropriately enough, steam-powered. 

Although the use of the device was plain to everyone, it was instead advertised as a treatment for a common female malady of the time, “hysteria.” It was widely believed that women would sometimes suffer a “wandering uterus” (Greek: hystera), which, if left untreated, could result in compression of the vital organs and debilitating neurosis. The condition could be ameliorated using a variety of devices that would induce “paroxysms.” Curiously enough, even though the stimulating treatments were widely practiced by the ladies of the time, the ailment continued to plague the States. 

With the advent of the mail-order catalog, early vibrators were first marketed, coyly, as “personal massagers” (not unlike the strategy of today’s Radio Shacks) and then, more openly, as “marital aides.” Not an uncommon household appliance, the “massager” was lauded by women’s magazines and one of its early retailers, Sears Roebuck, as the quintessential gift for a woman, designed to preserve her youth and keep her neurotic outbursts at bay. 

This stigma-free carte blanche was not to last the Great War, however. Moving pictures were all the rage, and it was only a matter of time before the oldest and most basic form of entertainment made the leap into the new medium. Erotic movies soon abounded, bringing to the big screen irrefutable proof of the nature (and non-medicinal results) of massager use. The social pretense was inevitably and irrevocably obliterated, and female sexuality was transformed from a robust freedom behind a translucent veil of propriety into what was, once again, an elephant in the bedroom.
