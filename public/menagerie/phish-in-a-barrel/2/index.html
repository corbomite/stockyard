There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 3 ) : Phish in a Barrel, by J.P. Gorman

It would be inaccurate to say that everyone slinging something was on tour with the band. Bud and Marla, for instance, were from just up the road in East Troy, Wisconsin and wanted nothing more to do with this particular concert than to sell their booze, this lot full of people being an almost exact facsimile of the proverbial fish in a barrel and the Jell-O shots being a cheap-to-produce, easy sale. Everyone had money in his or her pocket, everyone was looking to have a good time, and no one gave too much of a damn about flinging their dead presidents around like confetti, so long as it kept a nice buzz going.

And all of the above activity took place before the people selling the heady narcotic stuff showed up. Once they rolled past, money ceased to be an object of any consequence, save a means to the end of obtaining whatever was in the quiet guy’s pocket and flying off to wherever that lead, as though each dollar were magnetically attracted to this other man’s wallet and simple physics demanded their reunion.

This fellow could have bought a nice piece of land or a mid-size car with the dollars he pulled in, but there was little doubt where that money was going: first into his stomach, next towards the purchase of a ticket, then into his gas tank and on to the next show. Wherever Phish goes, so does this traveling circus of underground commerce, an economy unto itself, hustling and bustling and maintaining equilibrium due to the sheer volume of opportunity available when forty-thousand people camp out for several hours in the middle of nowhere, waiting on a show to start.

Is this rabid commerce some sort of violation? Some sort of hypocrisy, in light of the hippie ideals of peace and love?

Hardly.

If anything related to those outmoded ideals actually existed, individual tickets wouldn’t cost fifty-six bucks, parking ten, and official T-shirts thirty. There wouldn’t have been so many high-end SUVs in the lot. There wouldn’t have been as many grills on display, nor as many spreads from Whole Foods. There were plenty of greasy and penniless people in attendance, but someone, somewhere, threw down some scratch for them to make the trip, probably their parents, possibly their dealers.

People attempting to escape into some “hippie scene” at a Phish concert are about thirty years too late. Commerce is the name of the game for Phish. These men own all their own masters, set up a merchandising arm that sells everything from hoodies to onesies, peddle top-quality “bootleg” soundboard recordings of every show in the band’s history for which there is one available, sell most of the tickets to every show themselves, and have been doing all of this for years. Respect is in order: these guys carved out a megalopolis unto themselves and to their talent, and people pay good money to be involved with it.

Wherever Phish goes, so does this traveling circus of underground commerce, an economy unto itself, hustling and bustling and maintaining equilibrium due to the sheer volume of opportunity…

This being the case, it is of little use bemoaning the scene surrounding the band being hijacked by individual commercial interests. Spending money is now as essential to life as ATP, and it’s best to come to terms with this fact. In the case of Phish, the music is the silver lining this commercialism’s cloud, the band currently sounding better than they have since the late 90s, if not earlier. Thus, by the time the depth of the jam following “Mike’s Song” revealed its brilliant self, none of the thousands present cared about anything other than that unique post-jam euphoria of having witnessed something excellent happen as it happened. The music lives, it breathes, and ultimately that is the draw, as well it should be. Everything else, all this free-flowing currency, is a direct result of what these men are capable of when playing music together.

It’s genius, really, convincing free spirits that it’s okay to spend money. Whole media empires are devoted to getting people to pay for the pleasure of living a so-called lifestyle, and Phish is no different. The music, the freedom, the communion of souls as these four guys jam together beneath a boundless, starry nighttime sky is available when Phish comes to your area, and it is available for a price.

Freedom, it turns out, is something less than free, as far as Phish is concerned. This is not unreasonable, nor should it be unexpected. The guys in Phish make quite a living for themselves, and not by accident.

Why, then, should things be any different in the lot?
