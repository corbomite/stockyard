There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : The Darkroom of Angela Geis, by Mikayla Lynch
The scorched remains of a burnt mausoleum. Art/Photography Credit: Angela Geis

M: Have you experienced what you’d call a paranormal presence while taking photographs?

A: I have deliberately visited the most haunted cemeteries in Chicago – they’re mostly in the Northwest suburbs – and I have always come up short. Yet a strange event occurred in one cemetery I visited, where people place rocks on top of the headstones to commemorate their presence. My husband and I were walking through the graveyard, deserted at the time, and saw a large pile of rocks sitting atop this one headstone. We walked further into the brush, about a mile or so. When we returning, the rocks were sitting on another headstone.

M: Were you spooked?

Angela smiles softly and shrugs her shoulders in silence.

M: I want to talk more about photography as a mediator between the spiritual world and the mortal world. There is a film, called The Omen, in which a photojournalist takes pictures of the various characters associated with a child who is the progeny of the Devil. Each photograph exhibits some anomaly that, it is discovered, depicts the subject’s impending death. For example, prior to a character’s decapitation, photographs of him show a thin white line, almost bladelike, separating the man’s head from his body. The film is depicting the communication, here a foreboding communication, of the paranormal through art (specifically photography). Is it your belief that such events occur, in whatever sense and on what ever scale?

A: Of course. I’m very open to all sorts of the unknown, and I’m not scared of it: I embrace the abnormal. While I have no interest in this city’s so-called occult scene, however, I do know a lot about the dark history of Chicago. I’ve been in the Excalibur, which is now a nightclub but was used as a onetime morgue when a great ship went down in our river. I was there during the day, with a friend of mine who was filling out a job application, and I could just feel the presence of something else. It’s not spooky; it feels like the cemetery. You feel like something else is here. It just feels like a lot of death.

M: And what does death feel like?

A: You just sense countless memories of the pain and suffering of these people. It is very heavy, but not scary. Most people shy away from death or can’t handle it. But cemeteries are the resting place, of course—their residents have escaped hell on earth. I, at least, have never found taking inspiration from these deathly places to be anything like a sinister enterprise. I don’t do the top-forty radio—I pale at the normal. In a grand way, life is very boring.

M: Your attraction to photographing cemeteries is unique; it is a muse unlike any other. I am curious as to what you want to expose in these photographs. What’s the goal?

A: I like to see people’s reactions. I am curious about what others may see in the same scene that I see. There is one photo, called “The Unknown,” that captures the front of a mausoleum shaded by pine trees; it is extremely dark even in the middle of the day, so much so that you can barely make out the faces and details it features. I put on a red filter to make the contrast a bit brighter. In the photo, which came out very light, the eyes of these faces are glowing and looking at you. I am excited to know what patrons will take away from this.

M: The interaction between the art world and the spiritual world is an age-old dialogue, sometimes literal and sometimes metaphorical, that deserves a revival—especially for photography, whose mechanical nature some disparage even today. That’s what your doing, and it is important. Given your visual impairment, which is perhaps here a guiding light, would you say you rely more on intuition when deciding where to shoot?

A: I like that I can see things in cemeteries that most people can’t. I like shedding light on the darkness of which most people steer clear. That is why I’ve never staged anything to appear scary: The world is there, dark and alive, and I’ll take it as it is.

To see more of Angela’s work or to contact her, visit her website: Whatever Photography.
