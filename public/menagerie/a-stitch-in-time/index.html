A Stitch in Time

These six designers, along with those of the out-going class of 2009, are the leading edge of the Chicago fashion vanguard.

by Jason Rowley | 17 Mar. 2010
Tawdry Details:
0 comments »
Volume I, Issue 1

This piece concludes our three-part series on Chicago fashion. Following an article on the history of the “Chicago look,” and an article on the Second City’s sartorial present, Mr. Rowley’s article finds us with the six new designers of Chicago’s Fashion Incubator.

Like fashion as a concept, fashion as a reality has always had its few constants: Paris, Milan, and New York. London probably makes the cut, and, as of late, Tokyo has shouldered its way to the front-lines. For those with their ears to the ground, however, the sneaking footsteps of consumers have pitter-pattered their way towards the smaller side of fashion, found in so-called “sleeper” labels and boutiques. (They wait, like the terrorist cells from which they get their name, to spring full-loaded onto the scene.) The trend, certainly a rebellion against the prescriptive tastes of department store fashion buyers, cannot be denied the almost 18th-century French overtures of bitter, blood-tinged revolution: Down with the aristocracy! The constants, it seems, are out.

There was consensus that while Chicago may be stylish, since no one has dictated its fashion trends in the past forty years, it is no longer fashionable.

Among the cities throwing elbows in this new fashion arena stands Chicago, whose most recent endeavor – the Chicago Fashion Incubator – seeks to provide six designers-in-residence with a comprehensive curriculum; the curriculum, while teaching the cut-and-dry aspects of clothing design, also offers more practical education in business planning, merchandising, and branding. The Incubator, sponsored in part by the City of Chicago and located in the top of the Macy’s building on State Street, also provides the designers with office space, mentoring, and – á la Project Runway – a large workspace, along the wide floors of which stand expansive tables, arrays of bins and boxes filled with surgical fashion tools, bolts of fabric, several dressmakers’ forms, and half a dozen different sewing machines. It was around these tables that these six designers took the time to sit down and discuss their brands’ particular artistic vision, the nature of Chicago style and fashion, and, of course, their and its future.  The class of 2010, announced in late February, are: Christina Fan of C/Fan; Donaldo Smith of Killian Gui; Jonnie Rettele of Nonnie Threads; Leah Fagan of PELOTON; Miriam Cecilia Carlson of m.c.c.; and Stephanie Kuhr of Dottie’s Delights. 

These six designers, along with those of the out-going class of 2009, are the leading edge of the Chicago fashion vanguard. Among the group, there was consensus on the dichotomy that while Chicago may be a stylish city, since no one that has dictated its fashion trends in the past forty years, it is not a particularly fashionable one. There is, of course, a difference: style is defined by how carefully people choose the clothes they buy off the rack; fashion is less direct. It is a synthesis of personally curated tastes, of prevailing trends, of the relatively static and the ephemeral movements of mode vestimentaire, which is not simply to say fashionable clothing, but the direction of fashionable clothing as a curated art.  If fashion as an art-form is influenced by the careful selection of particular styles, then the CFI designers-in-residence have been appropriately chosen.

CFI designer and C/Fan creatrix, Christian Fan

Art/Photography Credit: Taylor Burton

Chicago has the distinct advantage (and disadvantage) of lacking a design cliché under the weight of which it must flatten. Lacking this, we find genuine, unpretentious design, free from expectations of a certain “look” such that it can find comfortable haven in risk-taking. Yet, we also find that this free-form milieu produces both designers and designs that, unrestrained by defined curation, take risk for granted, and so Chicago fashion sometimes gives a knuckle-less punch. That said, the city offers all of the resources of a major metropolis with, as Fagan put it, Midwestern charm. It is, as Carlson described, a “sleek,” business-oriented city with the back-country rurality less than an hour’s drive away. “It’s interesting to see [the] two worlds come together.” Perhaps because of this, Chicago offers unique challenges to designers: “Think of the winters. You have to be really creative with outerwear,” Fan said. All nodded – groaned in fact – in agreement.

When asked as to where they saw fashion heading, how the playing field has changed and is changing, and how this relates to the city, the subject quickly turned to the recent economic downturn, from which most fashion labels are still trying to recover: “A recession forces people to look inward, to really look at where their dollar goes,” said Fan. “There’s been a real flight to quality.” Designers must cater to “a smarter consumer,” as Fagan put it, who doesn’t think “labels could get away with the unfair labor practices that they did in the past.” Economic crises tend to encourage protectionism, a certain drive to keep resources within a community, and people “now are much more conscious of locality, where and how their clothes are made. It’s been really great for local designers,” said Kuhr. “There are more designers in Chicago than you’d think,” she continued, “and it’s kind of romantic, all of those designers in their own studios, doing what they really care about.”

For at least the past forty years, as most American brands have moved their production to Asia, fashion has lost its personal touch and, as consequence, its individual flair. In their place we find an expectation in shipping dates and production numbers that would make Count Alfred von Schlieffen proud— and, make no mistake, the world of fashion is immersed in a battle. “You can’t find many tailors that will make a suit from scratch these days,” said Smith, whose current projects include the development of three-piece suits for his label.  This return to traditionalism is a recent trend in fashion, and how this new futurism, which looks remarkably classic, affects constantly changing notions of modernity has yet to be seen; however, in these six designers we see the future of Chicago fashion.

The world of fashion keeps spinning, the horizon constantly moving forward: “Fashion is spreading beyond Milan, Paris, and New York… it’s really exciting to carve out my part of Chicago, you know, before it becomes the next big thing.”
