The CFI Designers and their Work

Christina Fan’s path into fashion design was decidedly non-traditional: a Northwestern graduate who, soon after being accepted to law school, decided to follow her lifelong love for fashion. Her label, C/Fan, is perhaps the most established among those of the designers-in-residence. The line is noted for its distinct versatility, fashionable for women ranging across a broad demographic: from the mom-on-the-go to the hard-nosed chairwoman in the boardroom. Designed to transition easily from workwear to something more casual and then, again, into adaptable evening-wear, Fan’s creations fall just-so, producing a flattering look for almost any figure. Her spring line incorporates masculine affects coupled alongside silk and fur detailing, a number of reversible pieces for, again, added flexibility. Fan’s pieces are meant to be both wardrobe staples and stand-alone pieces, available and accessible season after season.

Killian Gui is the brainchild of Donaldo Smith, a line that has the admittedly tough job of setting itself apart in the arena of menswear.  Smith says his work draws its inspiration from both his well-dressed father as well as from his competitors: the clean, modern tailoring of Hugo Boss and Calvin Klein, the edge of Marc Jacobs, the cool refinement of Theory, and the Anglo-inspired quirkiness of Paul Smith. All elements come together in Killian Gui’s brand image of effortless masculine power, a certain intangible put-togetherness, the kind of sartorial intimation to which all business-wear strives but, in effect, so rarely achieves.  The label’s mantra, while perhaps falling a few leaps shy of revelatory triumph, certainly makes its point: “When you look great, you feel great; therefore, you become great!” Smith, of course, has much to live up to in terms of greatness: the less-than flexible market for menswear means he will, likely, have to carve out a niche from someone else’s territory. Yet, with both his vision for Killian Gui and his keen eye for what looks “right,” Smith’s modern interpretation of classic menswear promises to satisfy, as he puts it, the “expectations of [the brand's] fashion-forward customers.”

Jonnie Rettele, who “just fell into fashion,” also “just fell” into menswear. It began with her husband, who, frustrated by the selection of the rack, tasked Rettele with putting together a few, more wearable shirts. Out of this we find her signature blend of menswear utilitarianism and womenswear charm, which comes into its own in her womenswear brand, Nonnie Threads, whose interesting, detail-oriented take on fashion is only amplified in the menswear to which I’ve been privy.  Careful choice of fabric and liberal use of “heritage” design inspired such details as epaulets and woven leather buttons, akin to those on tweed blazers. The features, while certainly adding a certain professorial air to her shawl-collared pullovers, complete the functional “urban refined” aesthetic to which she aims.

Leah Fagan’s label, PELOTON, is a nexus of crisp, fresh styling, enhanced only by the durability of the types of performance-wear (that is, athetlic) it is designed to emulate. Fagan stressed the versatility and comfort of her well-crafted separates, which are geared toward an increasingly cycling-oriented urban population, clearly seen on the streets of wide-berthed Chicago. The cool color palate and clean, rounded tailoring combine in a body of work that is at once athletic, delicately feminine, and intelligent. Fagan’s design philosophy is concomitant to her brand’s name: PELOTON, “a group of cyclists moving as one, represents unity among today’s dynamic culture.” It is about constant, heightened awareness of immediate surroundings, “a collaborative and progressive effort” with another sort of higher consciousness, steeped in the necessities of social, corporate, and environmental responsibility.

Perhaps the most cerebral of the labels, m.c.c.’s rhetoric toes the very fine line between high art and pretension. Magniloquence notwithstanding, Miriam Cecilia Carlson’s work is heavily influenced by nature: the tumult of a stormy morning, the lingering smell of damp earth thereafter. As a Dominican University student, she studied abroad in Paris where, under the guidance of Jean Paul Gaultier’s milliner, Carlson honed her skills and artistic vision. Amorphous, more sculpted than stitched, m.c.c.’s couture is where her designs reach their most soaring, abstract, and iridescent—their darkly beautiful heights. The pieces of her Spring ’10 line, “Summer Rain,” flash with a subtle inner spark, as if lightning within the clouds of fabric. Carlson’s open-ended styling, designed for those on “many different journeys,” are contemplative and contemporary yet grounded in the natural, bucolic countryside of their creator’s homeland.

A brand developed on the recently resurgent burlesque and pinup aesthetic, Stephanie Kuhr’s line, Dottie’s Delights, of high end, vintage-inspired lingerie and foundation-wear began with a love of bygone days. After being convinced to leave behind dreams of curating her own antique shop, Kuhr came to the School of the Art Institute of Chicago after a stint at the Fashion Institute of Technology in New York. At the SAIC, she received her BFA. All of her pieces are lovingly hand-stitched and made-to-order. The brand is named after her grandmother, Dorothy Kuhr, but her work is anything but grandmotherly: lacy, structured, coy, profoundly feminine (and feminist), and smolderingly sexy.
