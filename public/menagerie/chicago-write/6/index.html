There's no place like home
( Page 6 of 9 ) : Chicago Writes, by Donald Evans
Keep up to date with STOCKYARD.

Facebook and Twitter

Of course, Chicago’s relevance as a literary city can be gauged by it’s importance to the outside, as well: by the number and caliber of authors (or their publishing houses, as it were) who pencil in Chicago on their book tours. Nearly every major author on every major book tour includes Chicago in its travels. Just this spring, Joyce Carol Oates appeared at Columbia College’s Story Week festival, Tim O’Brien did a reading at Oak Park’s Unity Temple, Peter Carey appeared on Victoria Lautman’s Writers on the Record, and Colm Toibin was at the Harold Washington Library.

Thirty years ago, Midwest Media founder Bill Young notes, authors coming to Chicago automatically booked appearances at Kroch’s and Brentano’s, once Chicago’s largest bookstore and the only independent chain in the country. That destination, in tandem, usually, with an appearance on WGN-TV’s Cromie’s Circle, which ran from 1969-1980, was alluring enough for any star writer. It is that way still in cities like Denver (Tattered Cover Bookstore), Portland (Powell’s Books) and Kansas City (Rainy Day Books)—star authors focus on a single place. But since the Kroch’s and Brentano’s closing in 1995, authors coming to Chicago choose from an array of destinations, often winding up in those far-flug suburbs to where a great portion of Chicagoans are hesitant to pull up their boots and trek.

Young and his Midwest Media – an author escort service that not only chauffeurs visiting writers, but provides publicity, food preparation, consulting, and many other details vital to pulling off a successful appearance – accommodates on average 450 authors each year.

The O’Brien reading was part of a series Young has started at Unity Temple in collaboration with Unity Temple Restoration Foundation, Friends of the Oak Park Library and the Book Table. It is an effort, in part, to create such a desired destination, and T.C. Boyle and Berg, along with O’Brien, have already participated in the series. Packed houses enjoyed, in one of Frank Lloyd Wright’s masterpieces, a free evening with nationally-acclaimed authors who were charming, enlightening, and entertaining, and in the end bought stacks of books.

“Oak Park is a town that has underperformed,” says Young. “It has a perfect literary demographic and for years has lost people to Naperville or Winnetka. The shift could be local; it doesn’t have to be that New York has to take over.”

The city’s rich body of literary festivals brings in big names, too. In addition to Story Week, there is Printers’ Row Lit Fest, Chicago Book Festival, Chicago Humanities Festival, Poetry Fest, as well as the annual AWP Conference, which rotates its way to Chicago regularly. Events like Lake Forest Literary Festival and the Parlor Emerging Writers Festival attract a variety of local writers. Donna Seaman’s Open Books and Lautman’s Writers on the Record, as well as Kogan’s Sunday Papers, attract writers to the city, or at least provide added incentive and exposure. Lautman’s show, however, ended its six-year run after a show featuring Jane Smiley on May 20 at the Harold Washington Library’s Pritzker Auditorium.

The Chicago Public Library naturally comes up, over and over, in any conversation about Chicago literature—it is a world-class operation that serves not only as host but coordinator of many readings, festivals, special events, and initiatives that help promote literature and our city’s literary prominence.

The connection between Chicago’s contemporary literary scene and its historic authors is perhaps more tenuous. Spears notes that the best way to gauge interest in literature may be to determine what books and which authors are still being read in schools. There seems to be little systematic study of Chicago literature, though there are scattered books: Richard Wright’s Native Son, Algren’s The Man with the Golden Arm, a fair amount of Brooks’ poetry, Upton Sinclair’s The Jungle, Dreiser’s Sister Carrie, some Bellow. The Chicago Public Library’s One Book, One Chicago program, though not centered on local titles, has featured among its 18 bi-annual selections since the fall of 2001: Cisneros’ The House on Mango Street, Dybek’s The Coast of Chicago, Hansberry’s A Raisin in the Sun, and Carl Smith’s The Plan of Chicago. (One Book, One Chicago overlaps some with the National Endowment of the Arts’ The Big Read, which focuses on “classics” and has featured Ernest Hemingway’s A Farewell to Arms among its picks.) Writers like Farrell and Sandburg, not to mention Edna Ferber and Lardner, are out of academic vogue, and the danger is that their work will become obsolete, or at least forgotten, in ways already true in cases like Fuller and Fenton Johnson.

Maggio, Gapers Block Book Club’s Literary Editor Emeritus, and Bond, its Literary Editor, have done more to promote the actual reading of Chicago literature than anybody else, perhaps. About 600 people currently subscribe to the book club mailing list, of which an average of about 20 turn up at the Book Cellar for the monthly discussion. Members include librarians, authors, publishers, editors and others in the book industry; but about half, Maggio estimates, are “casual” readers. GB Book Club has discussed and dissected 55 Chicago novels, ranging from classics to contemporary, fiction to non-fiction, serious to fluff. Topics have included Bellow’s The Adventures of Augie March, Barrack Obama’s Dreams from My Father, Niffenegger’s The Time Traveler’s Wife, April Sinclair’s Coffee Will Make You Black and Susan Power’s The Grass Dancer. Its April, 2010 book was Sudhir Venkatesh’s Gang Leader for a Day, in which the author infiltrates the notorious Robert Taylor Homes on Chicago’s South Side.

“It’s a city you think you know pretty well, but we’ll find out we don’t know it at all,” says Maggio, citing Gang Leader as an example of the far-reaching impact of certain titles. “It’s an intimate view of a world you didn’t know anything about.”

Maggio uses The Grass Dancer, a 1994 novel partly about a young Sioux girl from Chicago, as an example of a book nobody in the group knew until their reading, and Nella Larsen’s 1929 Passing as a book that had “slipped away from the public mind.”

“There’s a huge variety of writers and writing in Chicago,” Maggio says. “There is also this group of writers that are deeply embedded in Chicago, where Chicago is not just a setting but a character in their books. Their love of this city comes through in all their writing. If you pick out those writers, you can hold them up to any comparable writers in world cities.”

    As both President of Off Campus Writers Workshop (OCWW) which is a 64 year old group with 200 writers hosting weekly speakers on some topic of writing every Thurs. morning between 9:30AM-noon from Sept.-May each year, and as someone teaching creative writing - giving workshops in and around Chicago, I can attest firsthand to the incredible talent in Chicago and its suburbs. At OCWW we have successful authors and writers in all genres. In my workshops I see writers/authors expand their creative abilities having fun along the way. In and around Chicago there is still a sense of violent critiquing that goes on - although neither at OCWW or in my workshops is that tolerated. People learn from what works and if they want suggestions about what is not working, then those suggestions must be housed in a respectful way. Competition can be fierce in Chicago - but there is here also a sense of comaraderie - networking, helping each other, finding solutions that is palpable. In other locations that very comaraderie is muted by intense competition and ripping up each other's work. Nice to read about what is going on in the writing world in Chicago. Keep up the great work.
