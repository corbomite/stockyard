There's no place like home
( Page 9 of 9 ) : Chicago Writes, by Donald Evans

Other public displays honoring Chicago’s writers are difficult to find. ALTAFF (Association of Library Trustees, Advocates, Friends and Foundations) dedicated a plaque commemorating the centennial of Upton Sinclair’s The Jungle at the Union Stockyard Gates in June, 2005. But despite the creation and announcement of this landmark – in partnership with Illinois Center for the Book, the Lithuanian American Council, the Food and Drug Administration, Union Food and Commercial Workers, and the Weidman family – the Department of Cultural Affairs never mounted the blue and gold marker. According to ALTAFF Executive Director Sally Reed, all efforts to get the park district to complete the job failed.

“It’s pretty dispiriting that Chicago hasn’t memorialized more of its literary figures in public art,” says Jeff Huebner, a veteran arts journalist who teaches Public Art in Chicago at Harold Washington College. “They put up statues of Bob Newhart and Irv Kupcinet—not to mention Jordan, Brickhouse, Caray, et al. But not James Farrell? Ben Hecht?”

Spears writes in his Encylodpedia Chicago entry, how even “after poet Harriet Monroe pressed for and received the commission to write the dedicatory poem at the 1893 World’s Columbian Exposition, she discovered that belles-lettres ranked low on the list of artistic forms that Chicago’s elite were willing to support financially. At the high point in the city’s cultural uplift, Monroe’s ‘Columbian Ode’ barely sold, and the poet used the unsold copies to fuel the stove in her bedroom. In a city dominated by economic interests and lacking in literary traditions, even the most genteel efforts to boost literature’s cultural importance had limited influence.”

A lot of so-called Chicago writers came and left. Richard Wright is a good example. Later, of course, Algren and Bellow both packed up and left Chicago behind— few kind words to say about its literary life on their aged breath. Young commuted to Boston nearly every weekend for three years, and he remembers fondly the abundance of parties at which writers were in attendance, a phenomenon that also happens year-round in London and New York but not Chicago.

“Most of our great writers are critical of the city that chewed up and spit people out: most of the people in charge of commemorating our writers have a vested interest in cleaning up or sanitizing that literary history,” says Savage.

Yet, the example of Monroe might best encapsulate the difficulty and strength of Chicago’s literary life: She was a poet that not only produced high-quality literature, but dedicated her life to championing other great poets. She founded Poetry in 1912, and over the years the magazine’s reputation and importance continued to grow. In 2003, Ruth Lilly granted Poetry $200 million, and with it a world of new possibilities.

There’s a scene in The Man With the Golden Arm, near to its end, when Sophie stares out her apartment window at the El train rumbling past and onto the wreckage of her life and the street below. It’s 1940s Division Street – far removed from the re-gentrified and re-landscaped Division Street of 2010 – a scene set 70 years in the past, yet undeniably ours. There is a whole Chicago in our literature or, I should say, hundreds of whole Chicagoes, and these literary landscapes help define our past and present as a city. Chicago is a city with a soundtrack, both in its literature and on its streets—from that rumbling El, which you can still hear, to the street corner sax players and drum beaters, to waves crashing on rocks and guns popping near vacant lots.

All of this sound – some of it music, some of it noise – gets integrated and processed through our literature, and those individual literary achievements help us retain a collective spirit, a Chicago spirit.

    As both President of Off Campus Writers Workshop (OCWW) which is a 64 year old group with 200 writers hosting weekly speakers on some topic of writing every Thurs. morning between 9:30AM-noon from Sept.-May each year, and as someone teaching creative writing - giving workshops in and around Chicago, I can attest firsthand to the incredible talent in Chicago and its suburbs. At OCWW we have successful authors and writers in all genres. In my workshops I see writers/authors expand their creative abilities having fun along the way. In and around Chicago there is still a sense of violent critiquing that goes on - although neither at OCWW or in my workshops is that tolerated. People learn from what works and if they want suggestions about what is not working, then those suggestions must be housed in a respectful way. Competition can be fierce in Chicago - but there is here also a sense of comaraderie - networking, helping each other, finding solutions that is palpable. In other locations that very comaraderie is muted by intense competition and ripping up each other's work. Nice to read about what is going on in the writing world in Chicago. Keep up the great work.
