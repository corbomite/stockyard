There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Kanchanaburi, by Brent Katte

I say goodbye, turn down Song Khwae – next to the river and its famed bridge – and head north towards the guesthouse. After a couple of minutes, the road veers right and becomes unpleasant as it approaches the markets and the Allied War Cemetery. I stop for a minute to consider my options.

Dusk, slowly dripping, soaks the sky as I walk down the alley next to the river. No concrete paves the way; only rich, mahogany dirt kisses my feet, damp and cool. On my left, the bank slopes abruptly down to the river, itself obscured by towering tamarind trees; the right is a series of oddment-littered backyards, sometimes peopled, sometimes patrolled by dogs or chickens. A sudden silence clings to the alley, making me cognizant of the padded sound of my steps; it’s just about quitting time, and most people aren’t back from their labors yet. The insects accompany me, flies and mosquitoes; frogs have started their evening gossip. The night’s rhythm is beginning, this day almost over, and my mind alights from its diurnal concerns and approaches a cooler path.

I see them a couple of minutes later, up ahead, the kids. From a distance, it looks like they’re playing hopscotch, or some variation of it. The shorter boy notices me and informs the others, but his sentry actions trigger little more than a few glances and bursts of laughter. With enthusiastic focus they resume their game, not at all concerned with the big farang moving down their alley.

I slow down deliberately as I approach, in order to observe, see what they’re up to. It’s hopscotch alright, and the court’s in the middle of the alley, crudely carved in the dirt. Shouting, tossing, and jumping are three boys and a girl, all around ten years of age, in varying states of clothing in various states of decay. At first glance, they look pretty poor, in definite want.

There’s something inviting in their motions, something welcoming, something endearing.

After the initial sighting, they’ve all but forgotten about me, the lumbering foreigner, rich beyond their wildest dreams, perhaps tormented as well. I examine around the scene, take it all in, put them in the picture, put the picture in me, frame things. My mind moves to childhood and its bliss, its great strangeness to the adult mess we all inhabit some day. Watching them play, looking around, I’m thinking that this could very well could be their future—this alley, this place, this poverty. I’m thinking it’s more than likely – highly probable, really – and, if so, what are the odds of their feeling like as do now ten years down the line? I know it’s apples and oranges and exponentially complicated and not even a fair question, but I have to wonder how often these kids will smile like this when they get older.

After the second glance, or maybe the third, or maybe the eighth, I’ve stopped walking towards them and, hanging back about fifteen yards down the alley, fumble for a cigarette, attempting to make it seem as though they’re not the reason I’ve stopped. A puff, and I don’t see the timeline as much, don’t see them only twenty years down the road. They’re just here now. Like me. We’re here, the big kid and the little ones, playing different games, but playing all the same. And now I’m taken by the sheer pleasure that engulfs them, the utter simplicity of their joy.

The purity. The gratitude. How it makes me smile. How I am. Their poverty is not so patent now.

Halfway down, my pleasant observations gain momentum and weight, become ruminations. I’m taken out of their game, back into the alley, into the surroundings, all I know and think I do. And I’m elsewhere as well, back in California, talking with a girlfriend’s son; back in Seattle, watching myself; drifting in and out of memories, collecting the tickets.

He’s a kid right now, not too much older than these kids, and the only one I can truly say I’ve spent a considerable amount of time around. A kid. He wants things. A lot of things. He’s up and down a lot. Hormones, the tides, whatever. Sometimes he’s happy. Sometimes he smiles. But not usually like this.

I was once this age, too. I was a kid. I wanted things. A lot of things. An awful lot of things. Usually the wrong ones, the seasonal ones, fleeting ones, things other kids wanted. Fashions. A lot of the time I got them. A lot of the time I was happy. Satisfied. A lot of the time I smiled. But rarely like this. There was too much to want. More than that. It’s what I did then.

It’s most of what I do now.

I don’t spend too much time putting me at eleven years into this picture, because part of me already has before with others and knows the answers, the empty truths, the bare walls and empty spaces. But I can’t stop doing it to her son, however unfairly, however out of context. Brand names come rushing out at me, trends and hype—those absurd demands. The kids’ rock, I’m thinking: The toy is a rock and a rough sketch, some friends, a few rules. And it’s working. Back home the only thing that can work right now is an Xbox or an iPod. And then only for a while. Things will change. It’ll be something else in six months, a different thing he’ll insist on having. The scene will be the same; the props different, but the motions familiar.

As I get closer, the smallest boy throws wide and loses his stone in the underbrush. He shrieks, and wastes no time at all in dropping to his knees, searching determinedly. I’ve been watching them quite closely for a bit now, and have a fairly good idea of what his rock looks like. I say hello and get down to it, tossing my pack aside and joining them in the hunt. And the kids are kids. This isn’t strange for them at all, this help. Foreigner or not, adult or not, what’s important is that slate-gray oval gone missing. This is the simple truth, and it feels good to be hunched over in it, severed from peripheral distractions and concerns, comparisons and contrasts, investigations and variables.

The girl yells something, probably that she’s got it, and I note a twinge of regret when her announcement is confirmed. For some reason I wanted to be that person. I wanted to find it and give it back. I wanted to play a role. But, almost as if I’m not even there, the kids scramble to resume their game; and I’m left marveling at the immediacy of childhood and its concerns, grinning dumbly at the totality of their world and my aloofness in it. They wave me goodbye. I smile back at them, understand something, turn and go.

I’m walking back to the guesthouse now, in a falling dark, on an unlit road. I’ll head back and shower, have some dinner, venture out for conversation, drinks. It will be different now, I’m thinking, different because of them.

I’m realizing that I did play a role, however minimal or unsung. I’m realizing that they had their roles as well, those kids.. Far from want, they were happy; and I passed through that, took some along. I’ve been smiling for the last few minutes. I’ve just noticed. I’m amused. I’m heading back, and I’m all here right now, on this road, in this town, in this moment; that’s all. I’m not anywhere else, not even back in the alley. I’m here, on this trip, remembering why I started it in the first place, remembering to be where I am. Free, happy, where I need to be—I’m done packing.
