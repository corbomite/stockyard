There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : The Womanizers, by Jana Reis
Photo Credit: Licensed under Creative Commons

Whatever anxieties I suffered on the way over, I took comfort in the thought of finally looking like the woman I had always known myself to be; the dangers seemed immaterial. And so, when the doctor (a tall, curvy woman), met me in the parking lot and guided me to the motel room, I simply wondered how many seventy-five-dollar sessions I would need in order to have a butt that looked half as good as hers.

But then the door opened, and the scene came into focus. Inside the room, paper towels, syringes, needles (not used for the operation), and a gallon of silicone lay scattered across the filthy kind of bedspread that has become a cliché in motels renting by the hour. Now an inch away from me at the door, the woman’s dazed and wasting-away face shot me a warm glance but suggested that I was supporting her drug addiction.

Still, I lacked ten grand and barely could scrape up seventy-five, and I approached the bed and took off my clothes, just as she told me to do.

“So I’m not a real doctor,” she said, at the last possible minute, “but this isn’t that hard. We just syringe it under the skin. No bags, no surgery.”

A cough, and a moment to reconsider. (If silicone enters the blood stream, the effects can be fatal.)

“And so I can put the silicone inside your body, but I cannot take it out,” she said, trying to warm the air with a giggle.

After I rolled onto my stomach, it was a minute or two between the preparatory pen marks and the injections, and then only ten minutes for her to fill what she called my “boy holes.” When one side started to leak, she tapped my ass with Super Glue and called it a success.

As I stood in the mirror a few days later, the results were a promising surprise. I then knew that I’d let the doctor complete the job over another ten consultations.

Costing me one-hundred-and-fifty, the next visit doubled my injections and convinced the woman that I was loyal material. She then admitted to one of her addictions, pills, claiming that they calmed her while she was pumping.

Soon I was a monthly customer, and she grew comfortable enough to get so high as to forget the amount of silicone she was syringing, forcing me to keep count instead. Oftentimes, she would eject more than I’d requested, and I would remind her than she herself had said that she couldn’t take it out.

But the obligatory laughs ended on a dark afternoon when she informed me that she had AIDS and was dying of her addictions. Then I was worried about having not only silicon in my bloodstream but also HIV. While she tried to assure me that there had never been any blood contact, I resolved that my next visit would be my last.

And so it was, despite my own addiction to her procedures: I cheated death still another time and have been healthy for the several years since that last visit.

Nowadays, she’s six feet under the ground; but hundreds of other pump doctors fill her role in the black markets of my and every other city. I regret signing up for their services, but I’m mostly glad to have come out just fine. Many other young women in my community have not been so lucky.
