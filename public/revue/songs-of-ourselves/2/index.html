There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 3 ) : Songs of Ourselves, by J.P. Gorman

I began the decade an avowed fan of Greatest Hits collections and classic rock, as well as the latest radio-approved swill. By mid-decade, I avidly read Spin and could discourse with relative ease on Bright Eyes and The Libertines. A My Morning Jacket concert changed my life, and I came to despise the lazy sentiment of Greatest Hits collections. As the decade continued into its second half, I spent some time as a music journalist and attended my first Bonnaroo on a press pass. By this time I had dabbled in jam-band culture and moved on to become an “indie”-loving hipster. I forsook radio and big-time record labels, and was anti-anyone you may have heard of, strident in my belief in the purity of noise, well-timed falsetto, searing guitars, and the wisdom of the unknown troubadour.

Late in the decade, I removed my combat boots, turned in my hip card, cancelled my music mag subscriptions, refused to read the most-popular bloggers, and found a new appreciation for older artists like Joe Strummer and Gram Parsons. You could say it was a sophistication that took over, but there’s little sophisticated about a rollicking crowd in a dimly-lit club, about fawning man-love for Jacket lead singer Jim James, or about internal debates concerning the relative merits of post-Libertines bands Babyshambles and Dirty Pretty Things.

The only constant during this entire time was the obsession. The group of people I met in my first year after college (a group that included Matt and everyone else invited to our party) shared a similar devotion to new music. We dj-ed our own soundtrack, turned off the radio, and went to the record store to see if maybe that one record we heard so much about might warrant a purchase this time, and afterwards a listening party. Beyond this crew, most of the friendships I have made since college have had a similar taste in music as the scaffolding upon which they were built.

So when Matt threw out the idea for a “Best of the Decade” party, it seemed an obvious suggestion demanding an immediate affirmative. We grew up in that decade. We became friends in that decade. We became men in that decade while devouring an astonishing amount of music. Agreeing with his idea is the most natural thing I’ve done so far this year.

But then… a crisis. What to pick?

How to express the preceding decade musically? How to distill those ten years’ different and often conflicting personae into one ten-track disc? Nerves set in as I wondered whether any real purpose was served in recalling uncomfortable, unfortunate memories by virtue of unearthing these “Important” albums and parsing their best cuts.

I decided early to go with my favorite discs and leave it at that. One can’t call something definitively “the best,” as there is nothing more subjective than taste, no matter what publications or television shows claim. Self-absorption is ultimately the centerpeice, as is self-indulgence. Even if someone else agrees another’s #1 choice, they don’t do so for exactly similar reasons, mostly because they can’t: no two people share experience in any complete way, and therefore music will not affect those two people in an exactly similar fashion. A personal list of the most meaningful music of a lifetime or decade has no hope of eliciting such deep, profound pleasure in another. Attempting to do so is a pointless errand, so I disregarded the impulse.

Do I honestly believe that Marah’s If You Didn’t Laugh, You’d Cry is one of the “best” records of the last decade? No. It’s simple, straightforward rock and roll. However, it was one of my favorites, and a record I still listen to, so it made the cut. M. Ward’s Post-War was a part of my particular experience for three years while I sorted things out emotionally, so it, too, was included. Neither of these choices appeared on anyone else’s list, and this didn’t affect me in the slightest. Only one record on my collection did so, the aforementioned Yankee Hotel Foxtrot. Oh, well. At least my list was honest.

The first Bloody Mary that afternoon tasted delicious as expectations ran high. The last Budweiser oozed menace, and delirium ruled the evening. Each song of the eighty played that afternoon brought about a new memory, good times and bad, hopes and dreams abandoned and modest successes enjoyed anew. A vacant, far-off look overtook each one of us from time to time as we remembered what things were like when a particular track first invaded our lives: whom we were living with or dating, what our goals had been, our situation, and these juxtaposed with who we are now, our current expectations, and how much those had changed over the course of ten years.
