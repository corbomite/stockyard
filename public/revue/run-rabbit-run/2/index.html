There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Run, Rabbit, Run, by Amanda Hughes
Art/Photography Credit: David Krzeminski

Snot rockets are another brilliant example. If a runner out on the path should find that his nasal breathing is impaired, it’s acceptable – practically encouraged – for him to shut one nostril with a finger and blow out the other. I was running for a whole two years before I attempted this maneuver. Regardless of the difficulty my allergies gave me, especially during my first Midwestern spring, I would keep going, too inhibited to attempt a snot rocket myself. This meant, unfortunately, that I was a mouth breather.

At about 6:15 one morning a few months ago, I overcame my sensitivity and, glancing around to ensure I was alone, launched my first snot rocket. The next few moments unfolded with flawless timing and absurdity: The offending mucus was expelled from my nose with shocking volume and speed, flying away from me at what I’m certain was a minimum of sixty-five miles per hour. Instead of shooting off inoffensively into a bush, I watched in horror as the gooey thread stopped and, still attached, sped back to whence it came. The hideous thing smacked me across the eye just as a shirtless, six-minute-per-mile runner shot past me. Since then, I’ve developed an addiction to antihistamines.

This miserable failure to assimilate into running culture might come as a surprise to those who know me personally. I’ve developed a profoundly undeserved reputation as a serious runner, something I brought entirely on myself last year when I decided to run the Chicago Marathon. If I’m not a runner, I’m certainly not a racer, and registering for the marathon was an act of stubbornness and sheer jealousy. Each year, seventy-year-old men finish similar races, some of them wearing pink tutus. The marathon simply could not be that difficult if cross-dressing geriatrics can manage it. And, physically, it wasn’t. My time was pathetic and I decidedly walked some of those 26.2 miles, but the bodily effort of the marathon was endurable. I was, however, woefully unprepared for the psychological torture that accompanies running for six consecutive hours.

My first bout of unexpected, and uncontrollable, crying occurred around mile eighteen. As I was hobbling determinedly past a group of excitable spectators, someone’s stereo began to play Michael Jackson’s “P.Y.T. (Pretty Young Thing).” In my entire life, I’ve never given more than a few minutes’ thought to the King of Pop. I avoided TV and social media for weeks after his death, thereby avoiding the media’s non-stop death, autopsy, and funeral coverage. But with nothing but energy gels and Gatorade in my stomach, weary from hours on my feet, my brain had become porridge. I was immediately struck by the finality and sadness of Jackson’s death: he would never release another (admittedly awful) new single, and all pedophile-themed Michael Jackson jokes would take on a new, even more ghoulish meaning. In seconds, I was blubbering like a newborn child, my face red and raw, my nose running more than my feet. Carefully, I composed myself before the twenty-mile marker, where friends waited to cheer me on. They expect an exhausted, disheveled, hungry me, but the tears streaming down my face would not square with my serious-runner reputation.

I’ve concealed my inadequacies as an athlete from many people, but not everyone. My parents still actively recall my pasty, bookwormy, thoroughly inactive childhood. Shamefully, to this day I’ve only improved my one-mile time by two minutes since my Presidential Physical Fitness Test days (from twelve minutes per mile to ten). I’m fighting genetics that gave me all the benefit of my species’ large brains and too little of its endurance-running fortitude. After the marathon I called my parents, the people who share these genes, who understand my potential, to tell them my time. “That’s wonderful!” my father cried. “And you’re not even a runner!” To me, it’s a compliment.
