There's no place like home
( Page 6 of 9 ) : Always West, by Luke Rodehorst

8.

Signs denoting the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act usher us into Yellowstone National Park. The miles of mountain passes, all one-way and pocked with orange-clad construction workers, bring us to a crawl; but it’s no pain to take the path slowly. Pine trees line rivers that slope and cross back over on themselves. Pine trees blanket the base of rounded-top mountains, gentler than the craggy Tetons. You take it all in, wide-eyed, hoping to catch sight of bison and moose.

The land here also boils. We stop at a series of bubbling sulfur pools. Staring at the wood etchings of stupidly adventurous boy scouts disintegrating into hot rock surfaces, we learn we may either stay on the boardwalk or, by the terror of their melting faces, descend into a kind of hell. The pools range in depth, some completely clear and knee-deep, some chlorine-pool-blue and apparently bottomless. Naked trees, chemically stripped, breach the smoldering sand. The land sputters plumes of steam, and the air smells like rot.

A spectacled middle-aged woman with a dyed, red, beehive hairdo serves us slices of apple pie and a pot of tea at a diner in a town on the outskirts. We’ve entered big-sky Montana.

Sixty yards away, a pile of banked cars suggests a wildlife spotting. We follow the crowd and add to the tourist clutter. On the edge of a spewing hotspot coughing out heat from the center of the earth, a massive bison stands masked in the haze. Its hoof prints outline the perimeter of the hole in the earth: a perfect pattern, ceremonial in precision. It feels strange snapping digital photos of the ritual, playing a voyeur on a hilltop. The reverent bison remains motionless in its steam shroud.

From among the different loops that encircle the park, we choose the east side of the southern loop, deleting Old Faithful. Southern loop meets northern loop at the canyon for which the park earned its name. Here, yellow, stone slopes crumble down into a gorge that funnels along a skinny river. Through throngs of video-camera-wielding tourists in cargo shorts and high sock, we elbow our way up to the overlook. We’re all pilgrims.

Back in the car again and further north, ours is the only vehicle in sight. Sloping grass planes open before us. We’re stopped abruptly by a herd of bison that’s decided to cross the road ahead. Beginning as a small trickle at first, five mosey along, occasionally looking up at the mesmerized humans leaning their heads out the windows of their cars. The road is on a ridge, and we cannot see what is immediately to our right. From this gap, more bison emerge, the trickle now a constant flow. Soon we’re completely surrounded by dozens of snorting and stomping horned beasts. No progressing, no reversing. We wait forty-five minutes before the road clears and we can carry on.

Then we exit, careening down a network of blind turns without guardrails. Handfuls of elk wander the tourist-center area at the base of the descent. A spectacled middle-aged woman with a dyed, red, beehive hairdo serves us slices of apple pie and a pot of tea at a diner in a town on the outskirts. We’ve entered big-sky Montana.

Across the western states, shin-length, white wooden crosses occasionally dot the edge of the road. Each one a reminder of roadside death—recent monuments wrapped in red flower wreaths; old ones faded and leaning, uprooted. In southwest Montana, there’s a curve on 89 North miles from any major town. Right at the crux of the turn, where bright headlight beams reach their limit in the outer dark, a tight cluster of four white crosses flashes into focus. A moment later it’s gone. There’s been colliding here. Now our car propels its pilgrims forward, eating the road beneath.

Ever taken a road trip across the U.S.A.?

Tell us about it!

Flathead, the largest freshwater lake west of the Mississippi, stretches out across a mountain-rimmed landscape. Lilly and I drive through unblossomed cherry orchards to a black-rock beach where I sift through stones and select those most appropriate for skipping: flat, rounded edges, heavy enough not to coast away in the wind. Despite my careful selection, the rough waters bat down my attempts. We wander the shores and climb a smooth brown stone ridge. Previous visitors have chipped away their names in the rock. We leave wet footsteps behind on the shale surface and drive towards my sister’s home in Columbia Falls, a ranch on the outskirts of Glacier National Park.

As we pull into her driveway, my seven-year-old nephew greets us with a handful of broad beans he’s plucked from the side garden. Before dinner we battle each other in the backyard with wooden swords, pretending pinecones to be grenades, the sprinkler a landmine. Lilly calls us in for dinner, but we don’t pay attention until she threatens to cancel dessert. We enter the house, grass-stained and sweaty.

The next day we ride horses at the ranch, where my sister trains an array of breeds: Icelandics, Andalusians, and an American Quarter Horse. Twice a day, with rake-like scoops, we shovel horseshit from the stable pen outside the barn and dump it into buckets with twine handles. The horses stare at us as we suffer this task, their ears raised, tails swatting flies. We empty the heaping buckets into a container attached to the back of a John Deer. The shit dusts up as we pour.

“Watch your shoes,” my sister laughs down from the driver’s seat of the tractor. She hits the ignition, and the tractor roars and clamors away, spewing tufts of dry waste.

That evening we drink beers around a campfire and toast bananas filled with chocolate. My nephew makes fun of me for spooking at the sight of a snake we disturb after turning over a rock. Dusk settles, and the cry of coyotes near the pasture pierces the air. We wander to the upper farm and perch ourselves on a wooden fence to watch the farm dogs chase away the invaders. The horses huddle together in a tight circle, facing outwards.

Before we go to bed, my brother-in-law tells a hunting story that involves stalking a bull elk for hours, shooting it dead with an arrow to the heart, and packing down his horse with the meat, only to have the carcass-bound beast cascade down the side of a mountain in the dark. Both horse and hunter miraculously survive the night, camping near a mountain stream. The antlers, each the size of my outstretched arms, proudly jut out from the wall on which they are mounted, cradling this story. Son looks up at father telling the tale with wide blue eyes, his head bobbing, fighting sleep, clinging to words and images he’ll retell to his own son.
