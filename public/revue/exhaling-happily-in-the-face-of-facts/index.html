Exhaling Happily in the Face of Facts

It is we, the nation’s radioactive supply of tobacco users, that eroded the ozone layer and killed off the California Grizzly Bear…. Indeed, the savviest of historians agree that the Great Chicago Fire was caused by a carelessly tossed cigarette.
by Igor Sadovyi | 27 Jul. 2009
Tawdry Details:
4 comments »
Volume I, Issue 1

Why do you smoke? Don’t you know that …” is a phrase that accosts me no less than three times a week on sidewalks, porches, and patios everywhere (I’m resigned to the fact that there can be no escaping it). Crafting what they consider a flawless case against my tendency to enjoy a cigarette, or ten, after every one of the most minuscule activities throughout the day, zealous non-smokers always presume that their indictment of smoking will best all the excellent, well-reasoned arguments I have heard over the years and will finally convince me to kick what is, in my mother’s judgment, the reason I shouldn’t have been born. I have abandoned the attempt to converse rationally with my accusers, choosing instead to revel in my denial of their logic; as they begin to quiver in frustration, feign a consumptive cough, and dart away to the nearest farmer’s market, I feel I’ve somehow won the argument.

As ingenious as my defenses of smoking can be, they have been impotent in convincing the great state of Illinois that its lawmakers are running a tragicomical, Dantean hegemony. The governor’s office will have you believe that smoking is the reason why denizens of Chicago cannot count the stars at night. Of course, the 200-million carbon-emitting vehicles in the United States, the half-million factories that are deemed so essential to preserving the American way of life, and the country’s infinitely large reserve of methane-pumping cattle and obese persons have nothing to do with pollution. It is we, the nation’s radioactive supply of tobacco users, that eroded the ozone layer and killed off the California Grizzly Bear. Contrary to popular belief, the Exxon Valdez was carrying petroleum to fuel Zippo lighters, making smokers indirectly responsible for that environmental disaster. Indeed, the savviest of historians agree that the Great Chicago Fire was caused by a carelessly tossed cigarette. Smoking is ruining the environment, we are told by our Birkenstock-bedight brethren. Yet the discussion of cigarette smoke’s impact on the environment has never qualified for the agendas of Greenpeace and People Against Smoking (PAS); their main gripe against cigarettes, which they view as an extravagant waste of paper, is that they misuse trees (From personal experience, however, I find that these people are never opposed to smoking trees of a different kind).

It is unnecessary to expound on the health risks facing the smoker: Every one of them understands that cigarettes contain carcinogens that increase the risk of cancers, contribute to the development of emphysema, etcetera, etcetera, etcetera ad nauseum. For hundreds of years, the scientific community has bludgeoned the general public with intimidating statistics about the dangers of burning and inhaling the great spice of Virginia. One need only pick up a pack of cigarettes to be rudely reminded, in a garishly out-of-place font, that “Smoking kills” and “Smoking seriously harms you and others around you.” Printed on a billboard-sized white label, defacing the graceful design of the cigarette pack whose perfection has required hundreds of years of the most thoughtful marketing, these warnings are intended to make you quit. Nevertheless, even though the pack I am currently nursing has reminded me nine times that “Smoking causes impotency in men and fetal complications in women,” I have not once reconsidered lighting the nine cigarettes I have savored today. The warning is less a friendly health alert than a shaking, foaming-at-the-mouth decree of damnation as hurled from a pulpit; it’s perhaps for this reason that neither I nor any smoker I know has deigned to heed these labels. The anti-smoking commercials now plaguing television achieve a similar effect (i.e., complete ineffectuality). Even the throatless gentleman who, through the whistling falsetto of a tracheotomy, warbles that “you don’t always die from tobacco” is no exception—for he brings a message of hope.
