There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : A Timely Pass, by J.P. Gorman
Photo Credit: IN.com

But there was a path of least resistance available, a way for James to dominate without having to demand greatness of himself, without having to bear anyone’s hopes and dreams on his Atlan shoulders. An opportunity developed that allowed LeBron to be little more than a prominent foot soldier, deflecting the pressures of hero worship and instead spending the next five years throwing alley-oops to his cronies as they routinely annihilate lesser competition.

James probably doesn’t view his decision to sign with the Miami Heat and join forces with Dwyane Wade and Chris Bosh, the top two other members of this staggering free-agent class, as a deflation of his potential. In his mind, he’s presumably still the league’s best player, only now he has backup. Perhaps he grew weary of being counted-on and of coming up short, of being pressed and hammered on his quest for laurels and historicity. Maybe he simply wanted to take it easy and shoot the breeze, enjoy the end of his twenties, and win in the method of every happily mediocre American.

Air-conditioning. Television. Computers. Cell phones. Smart phones. The Internet. Facebook. The great guiding moral of American life in the last half-century has been comfort. The impulse is intelligible, of course. Who would want to live on a sun-scorched desert plain without A/C or running water? Why should anyone choose to be lonely, when they can be part of a vast online social network? Information is everywhere, and convenience is essential. Each individual American, if he chooses, is the warm center of his own universe, everything delivered for a price, and so much distraction providing an easy excuse for people to expect less of themselves, to skate, to float, to let others break their backs breaking ground.

LeBron James is no different. While he sees himself as a basketball player, his stated goal is to become a “global icon,” his profession simply a means to enter the consciousness of every man, woman, and child on planet Earth. There was a time when athletes became icons because of the championships on their resumes. LeBron, on the other hand, was always the best at everything he attempted prior to entering the NBA. Because of this, he matured not into a winner but into a businessman devoted to selling LeBron James, the Best at What He Does. He’s a huckster with hops, and little more. Gargantuan, gravity-defying jumps, but a racketeer all the same.

The television special on which he announced his decision (a program titled “The Decision”) was an hour of painfully self-unaware, uncomfortably narcissistic spectacle that managed to garner the third-highest ratings of any show appearing on cable television in 2010. He slapped around softball questions for the show’s first thirty minutes, promoting the LeBron brand while ESPN raked in advertising dollars, before alerting the world that the next stop on his tour of global domination would be Miami, to play for the Heat, alongside Olympic teammates and fellow All-Stars Wade and Bosh, the one destination that would demand the least from him as an individual, and by proxy the one destination that will hurt his legacy and brand the most.

James will succeed in Miami, but no one, except for Heat fans, will be half as moved because he did it with so much buttressing, because he leaped off his pedestal, because “King James” ultimately abdicated his throne for a life of democratic deferment. James could average a triple-double over the course of the coming season, something only one man has done in the NBA’s long history, but few would notice. Everything he accomplishes will mean a shred of what it would have had he done so in New York, or Chicago, or Cleveland, franchises whose success would have depended almost exclusively on James approaching his limitless potential.

He could have made a home in Valhalla, joining in legend the likes of Michael Jordan, Muhammad Ali, and Joe DiMaggio: transcendent stars, champions who defined their era in American history like no other athletes of their time. Still, while LeBron’s decision is a reflection of the era in which he lives, it is not due to any epiphany of the sublime. He had an opportunity to take the most classically American of paths, to leave home, go it alone, and become his own man, in the process also becoming perhaps the most important sports superstar of his age. Instead he chose the most contemporary of American dreams: comfort, leisure, a pleasant social life, and no great fall during decline. So eager was James to settle that he even took a pay cut.

There was a real chance here for LeBron to achieve the remarkable. Blowing it, he proved, beyond any shadow of a doubt, that he’s a metonymy for America itself: a plebeian in a superhero’s clothing.
