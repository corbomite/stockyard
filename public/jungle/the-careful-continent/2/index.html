There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : The Careful Continent, by Joel F.S. McMurry
Art/Photography Credit:
Dave Krzeminiski

The punishment meted out against Faurisson that summer prompted a public outcry in the form of a petition supporting the right to free speech—one that garnered the interest and signature of Noam Chomsky. (Indeed, to the contemporary viewer, the entire affair might be better remembered for Chomsky’s participation: reminding his French colleagues that punishing a man for “falsifying history” is not so different from the efforts of the Nazi regime, the danger of whose historicizing is kept bright and bloody in the public consciousness of the continent.)

Yet Faurisson would not wince: in 2006, he received his most recent sentence from the French courts for his comments during the International Conference to Review the Global Vision of the Holocaust (colloquially known as the “Holocaust Deniers’ Conference”), which was aired over Iranian television. The event – whose attendees included such luminaries as David Duke, the Imperial Wizard of the Ku Klux Klan – was widely covered in the American news media. (Coverage, however, faded two days later when a cell phone of one of three climbers lost on Oregon’s Mt. Hood might have pinged a tower.) For the crime of publicly speaking about his views on the Holocaust, Faurisson was sentenced to three-months probation and fined 7,500 euro ($15,000).

The language of the European Union’s decision to combat “certain forms and expressions of racism and xenophobia by means of criminal” prosecution remains vague enough that it fails to restrict its law to the containment of Nazi war crime denial. The wording is so broad that if one’s public conduct “is carried out in a manner […] which is insulting” to a group of people “defined by race, colour, religion, descent or national or ethnic origin,” one has broken the law. What exactly constitutes “insulting” is, I confess, quite beyond me—and I doubt it is much clearer to those who must enforce these laws. Holding a neo-Nazi rally seems to meet the criteria, but what of a man like Theo Van Gogh, murdered for creating a documentary critical of Islam’s treatment of women? Is he not guilty if filmgoers are moved to hate Muslims? What if his Muslim assassin had pinned a note to his bullet-ridden body threatening Western countries and Jews? Let us also consider the resulting attacks on mosques and Muslim schools, which in turn sparked retaliatory vandalism and arson against churches. Have not the filmmaker’s actions resulted in hate going in every direction? Never mind the perpetrators of these acts – who are certainly guilty of their directly criminal and violent actions – it was the filmmaker who incited all of this! We shall put his ashes on trial and condemn him for making a documentary.

Relying on the emotional response of our reactionary species seems like an exceedingly poor measure for determining criminal culpability.

Consider the following: If based in Amsterdam and not Kansas, the Westboro Baptist Church would be a collection of thought martyrs rather than a family considered so metaphysically diseased that their hateful slogans fail to impinge fruitfully upon our public reality. The distress they cause while picketing soldiers’ funerals cannot be denied (indeed, it is certainly intended), but the vast majority of Westboro’s protests are really nothing more than a chance for bored college students to exercise their skills in creating colorful response posters. My own attempt to witness a Westboro protest was rather anti-climactic, having failed to glance out my bedroom window during those scant fifteen minutes allotted for their work. Instead, I went about my day and promptly forgot about Westboro all together: In the absence of their political uniqueness, Westboro remains a sideshow to the main ring of life, a trivial, fifteen-minute comedy.

For those who doubt, let us remember that Westboro spent money to travel and picket outside of an appliance store that sold Swedish vacuum cleaners because a pastor critical of homosexuality had faced criminal charges for hate speech in Sweden. Offense could be taken in the name of free speech, but any ballooning claims to logic are immediately deflated when one aims God’s wrath at a vacuum-salesmen (or at the vacuums—it’s difficult to tell).

Words are powerful; they are the vehicles of human thought and expression. To punish thought and not action is to demonstrate a woeful under-appreciation of the sanctity of one’s own inner life: The mind is one place where handcuffs should not extend. Laws limiting the freedom of speech display the utmost arrogance of socially super-conscious statesmen and their amnesia of the 1910s and 1930s, decades that proved that laws nannying the right to expression today are no safeguard from social injustice tomorrow. Resolutions limiting what we can say and think are too reminiscent of book burnings and secret police and firing squads and gas chambers. There is certainly little faith left in us if we cannot be trusted with either the expectation of freedom or the responsibility of civility.

It was raining on the day I encountered that miserable old lady, who carried her sandwich board as heavily as her scowl at that otherwise forgettable intersection. (She had no umbrella—I remember that.) While the rain pattered on the windshield, I preoccupied my mind by strumming carefully at my fingers to calculate the months until Passover and my mother’s homemade gefilte fish. Glancing in the rearview mirror as green light beamed in across the wet road, I saw a group of teenage boys gathered on the sidewalk: they grasped onto the sodden edges of a piece of cardboard, holding the freshly made sign aloft. Cars started honking; people rolled down their windows to cheer.

Emblazoned with a withered hand wagging a twisted finger at the wicked crone, the sign read, quite elegantly, “Crazy Witch.” No stakes or dunking chairs were anywhere in sight, however: Unlike Europeans, Americans remain unable to stomach such pageantry; it’s too much for their constitution.
