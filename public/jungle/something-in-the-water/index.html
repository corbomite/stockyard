Something in the Water

The reason why Prozac is in the water at all is piss: gallons upon gallons of rich, golden, chemical-cocktail human piss.

by Ian Gronau | 11 Aug. 2010
Tawdry Details:
0 comments »
Volume II, Issue 3

By the wisdom of Hollywood’s supernatural thrillers, man must “stay away from the light” if he has any hope of clinging to his mortal coils during a dark hour. Millennia of behavioral evolution have taught shrimp much the same principle: for them, “the light” is an otherworldly realm of birds and fishermen waiting to scoop them into the ethereal forever after. Yet shrimp have recently been abandoning their sensus communis: in what would appear to be spells of ennui, thousands of depressed decapods have been willingly floating to the surface and awaiting the sweet kiss of cocktail sauce. What could prompt such mass suicide?

Something in the water: fluoxetine, or Prozac, as we call it—and it’s not too taxing on the cortex to imagine who put it there. In a recent study by Dr. Alex Ford, from the University of Portsmouth’s Institute of Marine Sciences, lab tests induced this behavior by exposing shrimp to the same levels of fluoxetine found in our nation’s river estuaries and coastal waters, where shrimp usually live and spawn. The observations conclusively revealed that, under the influence of Prozac, shrimp are five times more likely to put themselves in harm’s way, a trend that will have devastating effects on their population. And the reason why Prozac is in the water at all is piss: gallons upon gallons of rich, golden, chemical-cocktail human piss.

In 2008, it was estimated that over one-hundred-million people worldwide are on psychiatric drugs alone; [...] that makes for about forty-million gallons of psychotropically enriched urine excreted daily.

Trace amounts of the pharmaceuticals we use appear in our urine in identifiable quantities, and our conventional water-treatment systems do very little to remove these chemicals from wastewater. In 2008, it was estimated that over one-hundred-million people worldwide are on psychiatric drugs alone; and since a healthy human produces about 1.5 liters of urine every day, that makes for about forty-million gallons of psychotropically enriched urine excreted daily. This is 14.6-billion gallons a year being dumped into our water—or about 24,000 Olympic swimming pools of toxic piddle, if you’d like a colorful image. In case you’re curious, this quantity eclipses that of the oil in the Gulf a good 190 times over. Bear in mind that these statistics apply strictly to psychiatric drugs and say nothing of the caffeine, nicotine, sedatives, pain relievers, hormones, steroids, and other agents, prescribed or otherwise, that further oppress the deep blue.

While it’s true that this urine is subject to dilution and natural filtration in our water supply, the compensatory actions of Mother Nature should offer no real consolation. An extensive investigation by the Associated Press surveyed more than one-hundred public water utilities across the country and found that only about half screen their water for pharmaceuticals. In fact, the majority of these utilities admit to finding drugs in their water, conceding that, diluted or not, trace amounts of a wide variety of drugs pollute the water we drink. It has yet to be shown that our tap water is harming us, but it has been proven that these trace amounts have negative effects on human cells in a lab setting. Researchers are concerned with the prospect of prolonged exposure to these psychotropics; they aren’t certain of the repercussions, but they doubt the aftermath will be sunshine, lollipops, and rainbows.

Contaminating our water is old news, of course: we’ve been doing it for over a century with fertilizers, pesticides, and industrial waste. But the preponderance of psychiatric drugs is a different story: their effects, especially in tandem, remain almost entirely unknown. Meanwhile, the prevalence of these drugs has skyrocketed in recent years and isn’t showing even the slightest inclination of slowing down, so that the hammer will fall before we feel it. As is becoming common knowledge, unchecked pharmaceutical use owes its propulsion to two major influences: ethical slackening in the field of psychiatry and man’s time-dishonored quest for the almighty dollar. Look no further than your television.

“Ask your doctor about Pristiq.”

“Talk to your doctor about the benefits of adding Abilify.”

“Will you ask your doctor for more information about Paxil? Do it today.”

“Ask your doctor about the all new Effexor, because feeling alone might not be normal.”

“Ask your doctor about Cymbalta.”

How much thought do we really give this phrase, “Ask your doctor”? Since when did an advertisement become a diagnostic tool? Why are we being prepped to make a recommendation for our own treatment to our doctor? And on what is this self-prescription grounded, other than a minute and a half of sad-looking people and a symbolic serotonin diagram? If we are not prepared or qualified to make a full psychological evaluation of ourselves and prescribe medication, why do we do it?

This question is almost beside the point. The delimitation of depression, bipolar disorder, ADHD, and other irregularities is a gray, inexact affair; it is an art rather than a science. While there can be no denying the symptoms of these diseases, that certain of these effects culminate in an exclusive, definitive condition that is effectively treated by an exclusive, definitive amount of one chemical and not another is still a vexed subject.
