There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Reversing Discrimination, by Jacob Malone

The prevailing problem of this case concerns, of course, the way in which we talk about discrimination. The “New Haven 20″ were not victims of discrimination: They were victims of “reverse discrimination” (a term that, tellingly, never made it to the Supreme Court’s printed opinion). In our social understanding, it seems, discrimination is an act reserved for the majority—is perhaps a defining characteristic of what being the majority means; and being discriminated-against is a phenomenon reserved for those groups which lack power. Minorities cannot discriminate because they are not in power; they can only respond to or reverse the discrimination that comes from “above.” By the same token, the majority cannot be discriminated against—or, at least, in no substantive way. Not only does “reverse discrimination” suggest that the majority is somehow impervious to disenfranchisement: At least on the level of discourse, it keeps the minority at the periphery, denies them the power to discriminate without modifiers.

Most frightening is not the fact that problems with discrimination and power still exist in our society, but that a term like “reverse discrimination” can quietly glide by on bright, red-and-blue ticker tapes at the bottom of 24-hour news networks without ever so much as a consideration of its implications. Keith Olbermann has never growled about it; Lou Dobbs has never got indignant about it; O’Reilly has never screamed about it. The “New Haven 20″ were the victims of reverse discrimination, end of discussion. That’s fair and balanced from the best news networks on television.

The use of the term is not simply indicative of unmeasured, unintelligent writing; it is indicative of an underlying, sinister sensibility that fails to be called into question because, in the days of affirmative action and minority political appointments, racism is viewed as a problem of the twentieth century—and I mean racism in the comprehensive sense. With minorities nowadays filling high offices, the great practical problems of racism and discrimination have seemingly faded into the dark oblivion of our past. And yet, Presidencies and Supreme Court nominations only treat the emergent symptoms of a greater illness. The discourse, the psychology, and the sensibility of discrimination lies most fundamentally in language, something that Feminists have understood for a long time now. If we can’t begin to understand this, perhaps the Obamas and Sotomayors of the world will lull us into the false sense that “we have overcome.”

If that’s not a case of disparate-impact discrimination, I don’t know what is.
