Midnight Oil: Depot

The 24-hour ramshackle restaurant beckons late-night partiers and stumbling college students searching for another bad decision to shove down their gullets. Depot, provided that your blood alcohol level is well over the legal limit, will not disappoint.

by Martha Munn | 15 Jul. 2009
Tawdry Details:
6 comments »
Volume I, Issue 1

Your head is killing you. Your throat is dry. You crack a heavy eyelid; nothing seems familiar. This is not your bed. Where are you? What did you do last night? And why does your mouth taste like… onions?

Most Southside residents will recognize the acrid morning-after taste of Maxwell Street Depot. “Depot,” in affectionate shorthand, is the last stop for many a party train. The 24-hour ramshackle restaurant, which sits nearly beneath the barbed-wire-lined Metra tracks at West 31st and Canal streets, beckons late-night partiers and stumbling college students searching for another bad decision to shove down their gullets. Depot, provided that your blood alcohol level is well over the legal limit, will not disappoint.

Its cheery yellow and red d√©cor is rarely seen during daylight hours; Depot, rather, appears to most visitors under cover of dingy streetlamp lighting, welcoming them back from another wild night, serving up another helping of late night fried foods. When the weather‚Äôs inhospitable (read: nine months of year), patrons crowd into a small, glass case caked with deposits from the fry-stove’s greasy, though intoxicating, fumes and stand shoulder to shoulder with graveyard shifters and glazed-eyed binge drinkers. In the brief months of Chicago sun, however, Depot opens up its summer patio – the adjacent sidewalk – to spare patrons a brief, though uncomfortable, oil facial in its glass-enclosed grease spa. With white-turned-yellow walls and creme-turned-gray floors, the cooking space of the restaurant gives a wide berth to tubs of sliced onions and the four or five chefs (I use that term carefully) rolling Polishes on the stove. As almost every inch of the space is in plain view from each ordering window, all blame for the product is on your shoulders: you know what you’re getting, and how your getting it, at all times. As a space, Depot spares nothing; it makes no claims to grandeur or against the philosophy of what-you-see-is-what-you-get; it is nothing if not honest. The staff, on the other hand, seems wary of anyone wondering exactly where their food is coming from. Trade secrets, right? Ignore that man-sized freezer.

Menu items promise not to break the bank (ranging from two to four dollars), but the damage they’ll do to your internal organs could end up running you much higher tabs, depending on the number of “Famous Pork Chop Sandwiches” your sauced-up, face-stuffing self decides to indulge in. (It’s reported the record is four in one sitting.) Since everything comes with fries and a heavy dosing of oil from the trusty, rusty teapot next to the grill, your heart will likely be weeping by the time you clear to the bottom of your paper bag of food. As for the choice of oil-pouring implement, it‚Äôs unclear whether the teapot is classing up the joint or standing as a testament to the hodgepodge kitchen elements.

Hamburgers, hotdogs, and polish sausages can all be yours for less than the cost of the gas it probably took you to get here: roughly three dollars. You might want to get two orders, in fact—everything keeps the next day, provided you don‚Äôt leave it in the back of your car (at least roll the windows down or your car, and all who ride in it, will never let you live it down). Whether or not your hangover will be pleased to see Depot leftovers on the coffee table the next morning is debatable.

But Depot‚Äôs about more than its menu. It‚Äôs frequently the ending of late-night epics. Triumphant heroes successfully locate drivers sober enough to guide them to their Southside deep-fried paradise. Myths surround the restaurant‚Äôs origins, but the tales‚Äô roots in drunken rumors become quickly apparent: “It used to be a front for a drug lord. They laundered money through the restaurant. That‚Äôs why tax is included.” For this reason, what Depot might lack in gustatory delights it certainly makes up for in do-you-remember-that moments. A story unto itself, regardless of what may or may not transpire on your own visit here, Depot is an experience.

However travelers find their way, and in whatever state, it remains a legendary destination for taste buds dulled by Natty Ice 24-packs or ten-dollar Vodka handles. And while the food cannot be called memorable – if only because, chances are, you’ll have a tough time plucking this drunk-munchie mecca out of the haze of the night before – there are many memorable experiences that eventually rise to the surface, sometimes through the only hint you may have to your late night excursion: your breath.

Not sure what you did last night? Relax. Pop some Advil, find your shoes, take a deep breath and exhale. Do pungent onions and a faint whisper of mustard caress your nose? It was a good night.
