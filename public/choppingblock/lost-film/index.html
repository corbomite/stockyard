Lost Film

In the case of both Sanborn and James, we have a lost man settled only on uncertainty. The center doesn’t hold.

by Luke Rodehorst | 27 Aug. 2010
Tawdry Details:
0 comments »
Volume II, Issue 4

The Michigan Theater greets you with gold banisters, red carpet, and high ceilings. Picking at bags of popcorn in the half dark, you whisper out of respect for the man in a white shirt and scarlet vest clacking away at the restored organ on stage. The AC is a welcome relief from the heavy air outside. The cloth covered seat aches underneath as you shift your weight, readjust your posture. Moments from show time, the man and his organ descend into the theater’s bowels, his cheery tune falling into evanescence, everything swallowed. There’s a certain sinister twinge to the sound of an organ piping from an increasing distance. Despite the slight eeriness, for a second, you balance your Coke or popcorn or Sour Patch Kids on the armrest separating you from your tag-along and, with butter-slick digits, applaud the disappearing man and his instrument. These are the movies. This is the silver screen. Magic happens. You’re on the brink of escape, the edge of another world. But in recent days the movies shuttle you back towards reality and remind you of what you seek not to be reminded of. There’s no sure footing on the big screen; everything is up in the air.

At the movies, however, particularly when it comes to the most critically acclaimed films of the past year, there is no art qua escapism.

Uncertainty has been the dominant theme of the past couple of years. America’s weathering and sometimes withering through the economic doldrums, where storied industry tumbled and once-dependable American brands coughed up rust, has burrowed unemployment and the threat of foreclosure into psychic reality. What am I going to do with myself? How am I going to support my family? Even common pillars of more popular culture revealed darker undertones that altered once-unshaken perception. Tiger Woods the adulterer, not the golfer. Balloon boy the hoax, not the family crisis. Nothing is as it appears or what we once believed it to be. In such cloud-thick doubt and anxiety, we need an escape. We need the cinema.

At the movies, however, particularly when it comes to the most critically acclaimed films of the past year, there is no art qua escapism. The Hurt Locker, winner of Best Picture at the 2009 Academy Awards, chronicles the sand-trapped world of a bomb unit in Iraq. The seminal moment of the film surfaces after yet another adrenaline-pumped close call with bomb diffusion. Sergeant J.T. Sanborn (played by Anthony Mackie) confides in the fearless Sergeant First Class William James (played by Jeremy Renner in an Oscar-nominated performance) that he hates what he is doing in the army, fearing for his life and the lives of others every hour of every day. But even at home, away from the war and its daily Hells, he has no family. He’s in no man’s land. He has settled neither in the company of his war family nor in the company of his blood family. SFC James’s inability to endure the daily chores of a quiet home life upon his return stateside further probes the question, What nurtures us? What keeps us going? In the case of both Sanborn and James, we have a lost man settled only on uncertainty. The center doesn’t hold.
