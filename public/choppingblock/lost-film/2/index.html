There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Lost Film, by Luke Rodehorst

post a comment.

What do you think?
Do movies provide a worthy escape?

Give us your Opinion!

Staring into the barrel of the downfall of the economy, George Clooney plays Ryan Bingham, a harbinger of corporate downsizing, in another Best Picture nominee: Up in the Air. Beyond a direct reflection of the times (in its portrayal of lost jobs and related fall-out), Bingham is quite literally up in the air, flying from city to city as a grim reaper to deliver the worst news to unsuspecting men and women. Like Sanborn and James, Bingham has no home and no family close at hand. The one romantic spark in his life turns out to be a fraud. He’s neither here nor there, has nothing familiar to curl up next to, except the constant drone of airports and hallows of empty cubicles. A little more hope seems to pervade Up in the Air, perhaps in Bingham’s mentoring of Natalie Keener (a tightlipped performance by Anna Kendrick) and his world-travel wedding gift to his sister and brother-in-law. But Bingham the individual remains ungrounded, and the movie ends with no certain path for the protagonist.

Even the 3D, fairytale world in James Cameron’s award-winning Avatar centers on a male figure stuck in limbo. Wheelchair-bound Jake Sully (played by Sam Worthington) can’t walk as his human self, but he gains super-human strength upon entering Pandora as an avatar. As he goes deeper and deeper into the native population of Pandora, he falls further and further away from his assigned mission to infiltrate the Na’vi for their resources, and he is instead drawn into the mysticism and allure of the alien culture. The fantasy world becomes his reality. Jake Sully the man literally ceases to exist by the film’s ending credits, and he is fully embodied as one in the ranks of the Na’vi, casting aside all vestige of human life. Sully, Bingham, James, and Sanborn remain in an individual stasis where they are clueless as to where they are going.

When the critically acclaimed headliners leave us feeling bewildered, despondent, and hopeless, why do we keep coming back?

Perhaps this trend towards ambiguity is a uniquely American film pattern. Luca Guadagnino’s film lo sono l’amore (I Am Love), in theaters now, proves that there is disquietude overseas as well. With the perfect sets and exquisite costumes, how could the astronomically wealthy Recchi family be stirred by identity crisis? The matriarch of the family, Emma Recchi (played by the Tremendous Tilda Swinton), is a Russian beauty wed into Italy’s old, Milanese-textile money. A Russian peasant thrust into a lavish socioscape, Emma never seems at ease, even with the world seemingly at her fingertips. As further reflection of this conflict, Swinton, a Brit, learned to speak Italian with a Russian accent. In an almost Shakespearean downfall, Emma embodies the tradition-versus-new-wave, family-versus-opportunity conflicts that come crashing down throughout the film. There’s a picturesque surface that can’t hide Emma’s identity crisis, an inkblot in a glass of water. Emma, in line with the male figures of the previous films noted, winds up homeless, separate from family and cast into an unsettled present.

When the critically acclaimed headliners leave us feeling bewildered, despondent, and hopeless, why do we keep coming back? Why, weekend after weekend, do we take down fistfuls of the same popcorn while we watch characters on the big screen reminding us of our own uncertainties, which we’ve attempted to leave at the door? There is solace in the fact that George Clooney (at least in performance) suffers just like one of us. We are not in Iraq diffusing bombs, but we are there with James and Sanborn in an emotional no man’s land. We can’t attach ourselves to machines to become superhuman, but we can share Jake Sully’s desire to cease being trapped in his skin. We’re relieved in a way when Emma Recchi shatters upper crust norms, despite the fact that it means there is trouble in paradise.

Yet it seems too base to say that there is comfort in simply seeing ourselves reflected in the movies. Where the real magic has always lain is in the creation of the art form itself. These films were envisioned by real people tapping into the pulse of the times as well as their own individual flare for re-creation. They take experience and weld it into emotional good, something we can laugh at or cry during or, mostly, be puzzled by. We find peace in the fact that you can do something with ambiguity: It doesn’t have to fester; it’s bad, but maybe not so bad. Hope is melded with dismal reality, an aesthetic change is astir somewhere in plain sight, and this gives us something to keep coming back to. That, and the man in his white shirt and scarlet vest, the promise that the trapdoor will open once again and there will be music. Sometimes what goes down must come up.
