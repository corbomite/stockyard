There's no place like home
( Page 3 of 3 ) : Talk Derby to Me, by Jessica Rabid (née Magdalena Serafin)

As the match continued, so did the hits; certain jams had the lead skaters spending more time on their knees than actually scoring. A few girls were benched for minor injuries, and one Belle had to be escorted off of the floor due to a serious back injury. There was, once again, no faking here. The bout did end on a positive note, as Dinah Party was able to make her way back to the sidelines in time to watch some rookie Belles have their first shot around the rink. Yet the Fury was merciless, securing victory by a margin of nearly one-hundred points.

After the final round of cheers died down, the girls from all teams swerved their way into the lobby as Poison’s “Talk Dirty to Me” blasted through the speakers and the announcer, in a parting shout, informed everyone to “talk derby to your friends!” The ladies, while weary from the evening’s matches, exhausted every question any passerby might have had and signed over a dozen pairs of skates belonging to miniature fans. Another member of the press, equipped with a video camera and a microphone, accosted the skaters one-by-one with the typical questions. I was positively riveted but decided to skip out early on such enlightening probes as “How do you feel?” and “Did you think that game went well?” and the nail-biter “What do you think of roller derby?” As engaging as the interrogation was, I wandered over to where a large group of the skaters was relaxing. I commented to one on how it is interesting that even the officials have derby names.

“Of course! Everybody is here because they love derby. We are self-run, and we even pay dues to do it!” She went on to explain that the derby revival is completely grassroots in nature and that many leagues are run almost entirely by the skaters. “It’s by the girls, for the girls!” Team members do everything from managing the marketing department to hawking T-shirts at a folding table outside the seating area. The members themselves do not receive compensation; they pay dues that support the league and are more than happy to do so. Most of the ladies hold nine-to-five jobs, their positions ranging from waitress to lawyer, from high-school teacher to doctor. There is not the slightest hint of jade coloring the skaters, quite unlike their professional athletic counterparts. Such commitment in the absence of pay can only be attributed to a fierce love of the sport.

Do it yourself and fund it yourself? Some would scoff at a business model that shuns corporate sponsorship. Yet this organization is much like the sport at large—a successful contradiction. Where showmanship and technical prowess coexist, so does a highly organized league without disconnected oversight. Over four-thousand fans made their way out to the season opener. Over one-thousand traveled to the Pavilion tonight.

Most impressive, however, is the fact that many of the skaters learned the trade just weeks before tryouts. Several of the skaters I observed tonight had been skating for only one or two years. Self-enterprising, twisted sisterhood skating hard to hard rock? “Where do I sign up?” I asked.

“Oh, it’s easy! Just come to our next tryouts!” they enthused. Then they decided I’d need a name and looked at each other in conference. “Jessica Rabid!” they shouted, their enthusiasm high and catching despite the marathon workout they had just endured. I can’t wait to talk derby again.
