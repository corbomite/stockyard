There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 3 ) : Take a Penny, Leave a Penny, by Amanda Hughes
A rack of books sits idly at the Free Store

Art/Photography Credit: Taylor Burton

This concept of The Free Store is so natural to Fries that she balks at the suggestion that some may interpret the installation not as art but as a gallery-housed Goodwill. “This isn’t done out of a sense of charity,” Fries insists. “It’s part of my art practice. I like that you get stuff, but that’s not the point of The Free Store. It’s a performative piece. How could someone say this isn’t performance art when as soon as a bag comes in I put it away?”

Her statement suggests there exists a most complicated relationship between the freeshop’s artists and its contributors: If putting away a bag of items is an act of performance art, bringing the bag itself must be the same. So what do the artists call these people who could be said to perform the swap-shop in equal part?

“Shoppers,” Fries offers noncommittally. “People,” Sakowski interjects. Neither of these answers seems to satisfy.

In truth it does not matter what The Free Store‘s contributors are called; it’s clear they are the most fascinating element of the installation. As one observes the subtle shift in behavior people undergo once they enter The Free Store, the installation becomes a layered, complex piece about responsibility, ownership, and rules. When launching this exhibit and others like it, one of Fries’ essential goals is to avoid making rules for the visitors. As she puts it: “You don’t have to do anything here except decide what you want to do.” An interesting dynamic is created when people come into the store and are not presented with any protocol to dictate their actions. “The Free Store really makes people more helpful,” Sakowski says, “or the opposite.” Without currency, each shopper is in an equal position to obtain any of the items in the store, with no concern for what he can – and can’t – afford. Because of this, it’s inevitable that conflicts occur when more than one visitor covets a particular item. In these cases, Fries says, the two shoppers engage in a very rare kind of dialogue with one another, negotiating over the item (“Did I see it first?” “Do you need it more?”).

Some shoppers, set adrift by the lack of clearly posted rules, feel compelled to ask fellow visitors what the free store’s policies are. Surprisingly, they often get an answer. The apocryphal Rules of The Free Store differ from person to person, but they usually include “Only take something if you bring something,” “Only take what you can carry,” and “Only take what you need.” On a few occasions, when someone at the freeshop has been in violation of one or another of these rules, a fellow shopper has even confronted them, looking to set things right. These kinds of rules, Sakowski says, are not how she conceives of The Free Store. “‘Only-take-what-you-need’ free stores are kind of commercial,” she says. “It’s putting a framework on the experience.”

Despite the artists’ insistence that their exhibit is without definitive rules, completely open to individual interpretation, the installation is not without context. In the next room is Art Work: A National Conversation About Art, Labor and Economics, without even a door to separate the two exhibits. The installations even share an artist, Salem Collo-Julin, whose involvement with Art Work likely made holding a freeshop at Gallery 400 possible. Art Work is essentially a mixed-media, multi-platform dialogue about the difficulty artists face making a living in a down economy. While admittedly oversimplifying the matter, it is still not unreasonable to think it will be very difficult for visitors to see a freeshop juxtaposed with an exhibit about hard economic times and not allow the installations to color one another.
