There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Written in the Stars, by Beth Hougas

Then Julius V. Baxter (Matt Young), the captain of the U.S.S. Sisyphus, proclaimed that it was “time to bump some alien uglies.” This plan was contrary to the desires of Commander Hayes (Chris Rathjen), who attempted to dissuade him from an exploratory “probe” mission on a planet inhabited by sexually permissive aliens (The pun was fully intended and meticulously executed).

As the episode evolved, I was delighted by the series of quips that led the tale down a road not intended by the mewling infant who suggested the title. The sexually permissive aliens laid eggs in the backsides of their partners; and these eggs hatched variously into heads and bodies that combined to form fantastically grotesque children, which walked and talked at birth and nursed through their mothers’ “backdoors.” The troupe’s performance kept the audience laughing, which for the most part drowned out the uncomfortable, over-emphatic guffaw of the megaphone in the second row. Even one of the more critical audience members, who suggested that the “moments in which they were funny felt more serendipitous than intentional,” requested that he be quoted anonymously so that he may attend future performances without censure. And, for the less critical audience members, the unconditional brouhaha of the second row says it all.

The crew of the U.S.S. Sisyphus isn’t confined to their weekly Saturday show time. To give you an idea of how serious these folks are, take a peek at their blog (http://improvisedstartrek.blogspot.com), which details the Sisyphean escapades of their “less ‘enterprising‘ starship”—along with episode synopses, character bios (Lt. Fritz Fassbinder “loves Earth Basketball, replicated sandwiches, and illegal Romulan holodeck programs”), and promotional videos written and directed by the cast.

In my conversations with them afterwards, nobody expressed a preferred nomenclature in the trekkie-vs.-trekker debate. And when I tried to stir up some trouble by professing my preference for Star Wars, none of them took the bait—though Matt Young told me he’d once heard that Star Wars is to rock and roll as Star Trek is to classical music.

I couldn’t help questioning the cast about their trekkie pasts. Fiancés Irene Marquette and Griffen Eckstein were employed by the Vegas attraction Star Trek: The Experience before coming to the iO and collaborating with other cast members to create the Improvised Star Trek. When asked if his high school interest in Star Trek: The Next Generation had negatively impacted his social life, cast member Chris Rathjen insisted that it hadn’t, “although people who don’t like Star Trek have trouble believing it.” (Chris is right.)

Matt Young said that they try to make the show “good improv involving Star Trek rather than about Star Trek,” which makes it accessible to audience members who aren’t dedicated or even passingly interested trekkers. As I have only seen a handful of episodes (and only for the purpose of mocking William Shatner), I’m pleased to confirm that their show is the former.

Despite the exercise in anarchy perpetrated by the theater employees, the performance was worth the frostbite. Aliens and earthlings alike should join the troupe for what will not, I’m certain, be their final frontier.
