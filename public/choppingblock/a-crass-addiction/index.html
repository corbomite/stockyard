A Crass Addiction

The artful shot of Alyson sneaking into her dying father’s room with the intent to burgle his medicine cabinet is filmed from the inside: a cameraman crouching in the corner, an incubus with a boom-mike.

by Joel F.S. McMurry | 02 Aug. 2010
Tawdry Details:
8 comments »
Volume II, Issue 3

It is difficult to look aside when a middle-aged blonde in a leather bikini is searching frantically for ghosts. Fitted with a beaded headdress and a flashlight, the woman thinks she has stumbled upon some basement-dwelling ghoul or specter and whirls around in fright, twitching uncontrollably. The delicate composition of her cheekbones struggles to remind the world of a youthful beauty immortalized in black-and-white glamour shots, but the sallow skin stretched over these points of nostalgia bury that memory. Her eyes bulge and she mutters incoherently; the ghosts never come, no spirits are unearthed. But we knew that already, and as we recline upon our couch and neglect the remote control, we appreciate the night-vision effect utilized by the unseen camera crew that has followed this woman on her methamphetamine-fuelled quest through an arbitrary and abandoned basement. She is visibly terrified, and we reach for another potato chip, sucking the salt and grease off our fingers in absence of a napkin. The program cuts to a commercial break.

One-hundred and fourteen times, A&E’s Intervention has treated viewers to such cleverly, or at least earnestly, edited montages that whet our appetites for the self-destructive and ludicrous behaviors that will unfold over the next hour. The program’s trademark title screen, with its obscured lights, nausea-inducing camera spin, and frenetic music, is meant to suggest the perspective of an addict under the influence and artfully symbolize the whirlwind of destruction that the viewer is about to witness. Although A&E’s description of the show emphasizes redemption and healing, this reoccurring title screen reflects the fact that the vast majority of air time for any single program is spent documenting everything but the intervention. Commenting upon the former model’s paunch and watching her accuse her two adult sons of FBI affiliation and conspiracy is much more interesting, and far less uncomfortable, than peeking through the camera as the boys tearfully recount meaningless, but innocent, childhood memories and beg their mother to board a plane headed for a rehabilitation facility. As Hitchcock taught us, voyeurism is only fun when the dancer bends over.

Alyson was the first addict to be paraded in A&E’s circus, and I liked her. The network had done its best to sift through the profiles of addicts less compelling and found an intelligent young woman, university-bound and a former white house intern, whose life had been derailed by a nefarious boyfriend and who now fermented on the second story of her parents’ house. Slightly overweight, with poor skin and stringy red hair, Alyson was gasping for breath beneath a miasma of orange pill boxes, crack cocaine, and tablets of morphine stolen from her dying father and washed down with orange juice. Of course, she said horrible things to her sister, could not be trusted to take care of her father, and adamantly claimed that she was committed to birthing the progeny of the boyfriend who had started it all and who now, courtesy of the State of New York, wore an orange jumpsuit and diligently clutched the soap. But Alyson wanted help, and when the actual intervention took place in the few minutes allowed by the program’s producers, she immediately accepted the offer for treatment. One year later, Alyson’s skin glowed, her hair looked fuller, and she was sober. The circus had fed its beast well, and upon release into the wild she gave no indication of having leapt through flaming hoops for the benefit of viewers whose captivation determines television ratings.

And although these hoops are flaming indeed, delight in the spectacle blinds us to their presence. When the headache engendered during the previous night’s bacchanals forces one to wake, roll off of a friend’s couch, and crawl to the toilet in order to vomit copiously, Intervention is almost soothing. We may gather around the television and forget our own degeneration by cleansing ourselves in the decay of another. While we are residing in such a specific, yet ubiquitous, social gathering, the twenty-three year old alcoholic on the screen is an unintentional comedic genius and moral pariah. How can one whose breath tastes of whiskey and poor decisions not find pleasure in watching a young woman imbibe an entire beer during the course of brushing her teeth? But when A&E’s programming schedule does not align with that of our festivities, the circus masters are impossible to miss. A careless cameraman casts a shadow while following a crack-head down the stairs. An awkward wobble during a continuous shot reminds us that there is a human hiding behind the camera, stepping over a coffee table in order to frame a better shot of thirty-seven-year-old Fabian, skinny and broke, smoking meth. The artful shot of Alyson sneaking into her dying father’s room with the intent to burgle his medicine cabinet is filmed from the inside: a cameraman had already violated the father’s privacy and lay hiding in wait, crouching in the corner, an incubus with a boom-mike. And a young alcoholic is lying on the bathroom floor of a hotel, scantily clad and almost comatose; she gives her two male companions an open, tequila-soaked invitation to use her as they wish, and we stand with the cameraman in the doorway, watching.
