There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 3 ) : Heard Mentality, by J.P. Gorman
A flash of light in darkness.

Licensed Under Creative Commons

By the time of Cobain’s suicide in April 1994, music aping Nirvana was everywhere. That sludgy, grungy sound was independent no more, and the “indie” bands had to change things up. Creativity and honesty remained an ethic, but the ferocity fizzled. To be “indie” still meant challenging listeners and confronting expectations of what pop and rock music ought to be, but the music seemed to experience a deflation in the raw, spontaneous energy that once characterized it. Musicianship and compositional form dominated albums from this period by everyone from Pavement to Yo La Tengo, artists insistent on maintaining their creative independence but unafraid to mop a bit of shellac over the proceedings.

This new “indie” scene bubbled beneath the music industry’s surface, down far enough to escape the scrutiny of casual music fans and prevent explosions on the level of “Teen Sprit.” This was music you had to find, and prior to the Internet being what it has become, record stores and mail-order fanzines were the best (and sometimes only) places to discover a band like Sleater-Kinney and pursue that interest within a community of believers.

The mainstream knew about this independent scene, heard the music in the ethos-sharing “indie”-movie movement, and encountered the resurgence of adult hipsterism in various urban dives, but the latter years of the twentieth century saw “indie” rock remain distinct from popular music. No Doubt and Oasis and Pearl Jam and Dave Matthews Band and Bush and Sublime: these were the centers of gravity in the millennial days.

All the while, “indie” remained something the longhair with the beard working the counter at Best Buy, the one who did his record shopping elsewhere, liked but your average fan of rock and roll did not.

Nowadays, the bearded longhairs are everywhere, and so is their music. Requiescat in pace.

Critically and culturally, the most followed contemporary rock and pop music falls under the “indie” banner. Once a separate thing, this music is now firmly entrenched in mainstream American popular culture. The Internet and trends in file-sharing have usurped the record store and fanzine culture as gatekeepers for this “indie” world, today’s technology making it extraordinarily easy for anyone, anywhere, to know about the latest up-and-coming “indie” band, to see pictures from shows and exchange opinions on them, all for free and from the comfort of a home computer. Furthermore, many of the most prominent “indie” bands do business together, share the same record labels, and play at the biggest rock festivals.

Consider the Brooklyn-based, morose rock act The National, a group that the taste-making website Pitchfork.com, arbiter of all things “indie,” recently called “one of the biggest bands in indie rock.” The National is on an “independent” record label, the England-based 4AD. The band doesn’t necessarily sound like anybody else, and it has made moves throughout its career to seem ill-at-ease in any of the more traditional genres. Some songs rock, and some songs wallow; innovations appear in form and melody; the vocalist sings in a distinct, memorable baritone; and influences range from The Smiths to Bruce Springsteen. The National has the moody, happily sad, joyful-in-sorrow aesthetic to a T, and it would seem by most critical metrics to be as “indie” as a band gets.

Yet 4AD, while unaffiliated with any major corporate label (Arista, Electra, Interscope, Sony, etc.), is part of The Beggar’s Group, a large conglomeration of “independent” record labels. This includes American “indie” stalwart Matador Records, and The Beggar’s Group does business, in some capacity, with major bands and performers like Radiohead, Lou Reed, Belle & Sebastian, The Hold Steady, The White Stripes, TV on the Radio, Vampire Weekend, The Strokes, Beck, and My Morning Jacket. The National released a highly anticipated and well-regarded album this past May and either has played or is scheduled to play at every major American music festival this summer. The band’s upcoming Lollapalooza after-show at Chicago’s House of Blues sold out in two minutes.

The National would seem to be a poster child for independent artists, but the band is actually involved in an organization only slightly smaller than the major corporate labels; meanwhile, tickets to its shows are white-hot commodities. This phenomenon is not particular to The National: it is a staple of the “indie” scene in the new century. Similar success stories can be attributed to My Morning Jacket in 2005, TV on the Radio in 2006, and Vampire Weekend in 2008. Instead of staying beneath the surface as they once did, these bands, still classified as “indie”, are the largest and most vital of our day.
