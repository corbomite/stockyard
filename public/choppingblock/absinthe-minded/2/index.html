There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 5 ) : Absinthe-Minded, by Timothy Hannifan

It’s hardly surprising that temperate and anti-modern movements found a common enemy in absinthe. Here, after all, was a strong liquor that fascinated most drinkers but especially those giving modernity its artistic and intellectual shape. When, in 1905, Swedish laborer Jean Lanfray – a committed boozehound who routinely drank as much as he could of whatever he could – killed his family during a particularly intense bender that only incidentally included absinthe, enemies of the drink seized on what they called the “Absinthe Murders” as proof of the links between change and corruption and madness and absinthe. Within a decade, the drink had nearly vanished.

Slightly less than a century later, absinthe reemerged with the fall of the Berlin Wall. Distillers in the Eastern Bloc had been producing so-called “bohemian absinthe” for some time. And when Reagan, arms tired and nails broken, stepped aside from the democratic hole he’d dug in the Wall, western pop-cultural icons in search of a fast and easy way to position themselves as avant garde eagerly stepped through, to return boasting that they’d rediscovered “absinthe.” (In fact, their drink was closer to grain alcohol with a mere trace of wormwood added after the distillation process: little more than Everclear plus essential oils. “Bohemian absinthe” was really neither bohemian nor absinthe, and it seems appropriate that imitation poet Marilyn Manson helped to introduce the world to imitation absinthe.) As the memory of the absinthe phenomenon reentered public consciousness, Western Europe slowly began to repeal its bans; and, in 2007, the United States followed suit.

The prospect of experimenting with the real thing was intriguing, and I needed a drink after distilling three books’ worth of history into a few paragraphs. I neither expected to get more than drunk nor worried about losing an ear (Credible recent studies have concluded that the thujone content of turn-of-the-century absinthe was far too low to cause hallucinations or insanity and that many of the demonstrably non-hallucinogenic absinthes on the market now contain more thujone than a typical pre-ban absinthe—and that’s the last factiod I intend to inflict on you). Yet it was at least exciting to anticipate participating in the ritual. If I feared anything, it was that bars serving absinthe would be packed with scruffily bearded artistes clutching dog-eared copies of August Strindberg’s collected works and scribbling poems about ennui.

I began my experiment at The Violet Hour. The lounge, just south of the Damen stop on the Blue Line, is marked by a single brass lantern floating inconspicuously above the rivers of fixed-gear bicycles, ironic tattoos, and pop-cultural detritus flowing through the streets of hipster haven Wicker Park. The immense, high-ceilinged interior is done almost exclusively in slate blue and is subdivided by heavy velvet curtains. Behind each curtain – and there are many curtains, so many that the space feels hastily assembled and liable to turn back into a warehouse when the clock strikes last call – is a different atmosphere. Some areas quote a nineteenth-century club aesthetic with a degree of lush, postmodern grandeur that would make Baz Luhrmann blush. Others are so subdued, with an almost do-it-yourself mien, that it’s easy to imagine them as the subjects of an HGTV pilot—Design on a Dime: Bar Edition.

Of course, the attractive obverse of the arts-and-crafts is the hand-crafted, which is surely the best word for The Violet Hour’s cocktail menu. In addition to flawlessly mixed classic cocktails, an immense assortment of whiskeys, gins, and vodkas, and one of Chicago’s largest selections of absinthe, the menu includes contributions from The Violet Hour’s most experienced mixologists. One of these artisans was kind enough to introduce me to absinthe and its associated ritual.

Stephen Cole – a former chef who brings a foodie’s palate and passion to the cocktails he now creates – started me off with Pernod, possibly the most legitimate descendent of pre-ban absinthe. Cole presented it in a classic dosing glass, shaped like an oversized champagne flute with a small bulb at its base. To add the sugar and ice water, he used a see-saw dripper: an iron funnel, suspended above the glass by three spindly legs, that slowly drips water over the sugar cube, onto a miniature see-saw, and into the emerald spirit beneath.

Pernod is a beautifully deep, natural-looking green—in the dim light, it appeared the color of holly. As it combined with the water, a hypnotic transformation began. Shining tendrils of oil pushed insistently down and out from where each sugared drop struck the absinthe’s surface. The louche grew almost imperceptibly; as it did, the color in the glass changed from rich green to pearly, milky, swirling white. No other liquor encloses the potential for this mysterious, complex loveliness. It’s hardly surprising that the louche is such an important part of absinthe, qua myth and qua experience. (“Bohemian absinthe” does not louche, and so the ritual is rather different. One pours the booze over a sugar cube, sets the sugar cube on fire – really good absinthe, incidentally, should not double as really good lighter fluid – and drops it into the glass. Which process serves to disguise the harshness and imperfections of the booze on the one hand and titillate the yuppie tourist on the other.)

I tasted the absinthe after it finished louching; and, as it is now my responsibility to tell you how absinthe in general and Pernod in particular taste, a nota bene seems in order: My palate is beyond uneducated; it is developmentally disabled. My constitutional shortcomings and inexperience with absinthe made sorting out and naming its flavors challenging—but, I should hope, not impossible.
