There's no place like home
( Page 5 of 5 ) : Absinthe-Minded, by Timothy Hannifan

Le Tourment Vert produces a lovely, cloudy, complicated louche and has a more numbing mouthfeel than Pernod and Kübler; unfortunately, its flavor is as artificial as its color. As it finishes and the licorice becomes harder to detect, the liquor leaves a shockingly foul taste in the mouth. It took me a long time to identify this taste, which initially struck me as something like cheap deodorant or bad pharmacy cologne (I couldn’t be sure which bad pharmacy cologne, nor could I imagine where I’d tasted it). The absinthe was overpowering, puzzling, and so offensive to every set of sensibilities I could muster that I preferred not to dwell on it. So it took some time, and strength and courage, but I came up with a theory: This offensive flavor is something almost, but not quite, entirely unlike mint—a synthetic mint that one might find in an unbelievably strong, off-brand chewing gum. The taste fades quickly enough – and, better yet, serves as a strong motivation to take another sip – but it’s distinctly unpleasant while it lasts.

Also unpleasant: The comfortably upscale quiet in which Potter’s is best enjoyed faces the constant danger of interruption. At any moment, tourists from the theater district or a wedding reception or the hotel itself are liable to pour noisily into the lounge; and the beautifully presented (if not beautifully flavored) absinthe is difficult to appreciate under the assault of bridesmaids, Bears fans, and the Jersey Boys crowd. Noisily boorish or quietly tragic, these people do not make for good company—and when there’s a huge absinthe fountain in front of you, everyone wants to keep you company. (Nevertheless, with a little thought, it’s actually quite easy to find a choose-your-own adventure kick in an evening here, as the clientele segregates itself in more or less predictable ways. One night, I sat in a peaceful corner and overheard a woman – Caucasian; upper-middle-aged, lower-middle-class; jean-jacketed; strong candidate for owner of the last extant working Flowbee – shakily whisper to a friend her personal philosophy. It was an odd palimpsest of pop-cultural banalities superimposed on one another: “I learned the truth at seventeen, that you’ve gotta have faith, because love is a battlefield, so don’t stop thinkin’ about tomorrow” and so on. Delivered with less earnestness, it might have been charming. When I sat within sight of the oversized TVs at the bar, on the other hand, the woman next to me – an HR employee “in town” for a two-day certification course in some new software or other – asked shrilly, between demands of the bartender for more Miller Light, whether absinthe is “that stuff from Eurotrip that made them see green goblins.” Which, for all I know, it may well be; but the absinthe was not to blame for the appearance of this particular creature.) Yet between rush hours and on off nights – although avoid Thursdays, for there is live music on Thursdays, live smooth jazz music, which is not something I seriously believed could exist, but it does, and it simulates flawlessly how I imagine it feels to be slowly and deliberately lobotomized by means of a spoon crammed ever so gently into one’s ear – it’s a lovely place for a quiet drink.

Potter’s, as it happens, is the first place at which I became seriously drunk on absinthe – under my own power, not on this fine magazine’s time or dime – and I think budding absintheurs will benefit from a description of this experience. It’s easy to forget that absinthe is strong stuff: Le Tourment Vert is 100 proof, and the water and sugar don’t dilute it all that much. I’m confident in this claim because I, who boast a Garlandesque alcohol tolerance, was well on my way into orbit after about four glasses. No green fairies materialized, and I wasn’t any more inspired than I normally think I am after a few drinks. But absinthe does produce its own distinct drunk (which anyone who’s turned a glass of champagne into a really good mood or a shot of tequila into a really good story knows is possible): It’s a more lucid, clearheaded drunk; it lubricates the mind without producing quite as impenetrable an alcoholic fog. Which probably accounts for its historic association with creative types. It also accounts for the fact that an absinthe drunk can sneak up on a person, who may after four or five drinks accidentally decide that bridesmaids’ gowns from David’s Bridal look stunning, pass out on the Green Line clutching a bag of McDonald’s, and end up lost and bloated on the West Side.

Here, I suppose, is where I should recommend the absinthe offerings of one of these three bars to you, the reading public. Which seems like the wrong way to close. Part of the reason is that each bar has something to recommend it: The Violet Hour a wide selection of absinthes, Potter’s Lounge a wide variety of very watchable people, and minibar a wide array of asses off of which anyone so inclined could bounce a quarter. But if that were my point, if after all these pages and pages of sophomoric digs at these places I couldn’t offer anything but languid equivocation, you, gentle reader, could rightly wish that my pens dry up and my keyboard shatter. Fortunately, this is not my point, but the languid equivocation points us in the right direction: towards a common “thing.”

The “something” each of these bars has to recommend it is not quite absinthe selection, nor people-watching, nor glutea maxima. In fact it’s something more complex, and it’s the same something in each case. On a very basic level, they all allow the plate glass door between childhood and adulthood to slide open. But more broadly: Each supplies a way of interacting with a supra-personal historical culture that resists easy and immediate access. The Violet Hour explicitly states that its aim is to reenact early-modern cocktail culture by quoting directly, from the chandeliers to the bartenders’ sleeve garters, the origins and roots of the phenomena that turned booze into drink. This is one way to commune with a distant past: quote, reenact, redo. Minibar, sleek and empty, on a different method: efface, ignore, forget. And somewhere in between is Potter’s: gesture indirectly towards, try surreptitiously to simulate.

I hope this claim can hold a little more weight, because I’d go so far as to say that every one of the epiphenomena I’ve described, from decoration to patronage, stems from a conscious or unconscious decision about how history should be remembered—and that, for these three bars, absinthe is the monad that contains the entire process. And so absinthe at The Violet Hour is quoted and reenacted – pulled into the present, given a contemporary spin – and absinthe at Minibar is effaced as absinthe – mixed, ignored, saccharinely overwhelmed – and absinthe at Potter’s is sneaky – an unacknowledged simulation that tastes and feels slightly off. Juridically and mnemonically expunged for almost a century, this drink seems, in the process of being remembered, to have forced these three bars to address the problem of remembrance.

So I’d recommend all three. I’d recommend that this terribly complex historic, psychic, mnemonic encounter remain an exercise for you. If you have any sense of the challenge involved, you’ll find a pithy, thinky, dry disassembly of the moment of encounter much less satisfying than the opportunity to drink it in yourself.
