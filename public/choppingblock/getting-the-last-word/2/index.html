There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Getting the Last Word, by Sally Weigel
Death sitting on tomb with young man holding flower.

Even so, allowing one’s last words to be recorded by the jail, the state, and the newspapers permits every convict one final opportunity to be heard, as an independent of the state and as an individual, in the public sphere. The state revivifies the rights of participatory democracy moments before death, as if to prove, however perversely, that no citizen dies alienated by the American dream.

It’s true that Last Words is primarily a book about the history of capital punishment rather than the ethical nature of the institution. Elder organizes the book into chapters based on the method used for execution (the noose, the firing squad, the electric chair, the gas chamber, and the needle), which works to show how the last words have changed over the years and also how this ritual has evolved from a now-and-then religious treatment to a tradition as American as apple pie. The book portrays an interesting progression from the inmates’ mostly spiritual statements in early years to more personal, more political speeches as years pass.

With Studs Terkel’s recent death, Elder carries on the journalistic approach that the former historian mastered. When asked about how seven years of research affected him politically, Elder insists, “[I wanted to] let the history speak for itself. There was no political agenda.” It’s apparent that Elder’s personal thoughts on capital punishment are not the focus. Terkel’s foreword remains the only space where a political agenda surfaces in the book: he remarks, “Some of the inmates’ last words provide the strongest arguments I’ve heard for rehabilitation.”

When interviewing Elder, I couldn’t help asking him the very question he asks the reader in the introduction of the book: “If these are the most outcast, reviled members of society, why does it remain a cultural value to record what they say?”

Elder responded, “You know, I was just talking about this with a former assistant, David Lanzafame, who worked with me on the book. He said, in short, that he expected the project to influence his politics, to shape his opinion one way or the other about capital punishment. Instead, he said that it just made him more conflicted.” What an answer. Instead of delving into such conflicting emotions, Elder continued, “For my part, I remain curious to hear people’s reactions to the book—that’s been the most interesting, engaging part of the book’s release for me.” 

While the apolitical nature of this book was both well-executed and informative, I cannot help feeling most critical of this choice. Elder spent seven years dealing with this question, a position that could provide valuable insight into the dialogue the book breeds. All things considered, Last Words of the Executed left me wanting to see Elder take an independent stand of his own. I wanted to hear his last words.
