There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : No Plastic Accepted, by J.P. Gorman
Art/Photography Credit: Licensed under Creative Commons

This next spot may be Innertown Pub, on the southwest corner of Thomas and Winchester, two blocks south of Division. Look close, though, because if you don’t pick up the small sign hanging out from the brick fa√ßade, you may miss Innertown altogether. Fittingly, I had a friend whose basement looked a lot like the inside of this place: dark wood and a reddened light scheme lend the bar a comfortable, quiet, more intimate feel than you you’ll find among most of its competitors.

Six dollars will get you a Pabst from the draft paired with a shot of Jack Daniels, a fantastic deal no matter how you cut it. They probably have Red Bull (It’s almost a prerequisite for bars anymore), but there’s little point in getting cranked on Jagerbombs in a place so mellow. You would definitely be the drunkest person in the room, and, what with the bar’s close quarters, this would not be recommendable.

To say the room is small would be an overstatement, but it’s by no means large. A kicking jukebox features music to which you actually want to listen (The Strokes, Wilco, Otis Redding), and, as with Gold Star, there’s a pool table in back—but good luck getting a game. The bathrooms are like those you may find in a movie about heroin addicts—but these are more disarming than frightening, more charming than offensive. The ladies’ room directly abuts the pool table, its placement leading to many an awkward conversation between billiard-playing, would-be Casanovas and rushing, distracted women in desperate need of empty bladders.

The sign out front bills Innertown as the “Home of the Arts,” and on Thursdays they have an open-mic night at which you can hear everything from singer-songwriters working out new material to guys with acoustics giving their take on Bob Marley, to older gentlemen with sheet music laying down finger-picked jazz that goes over the heads of everyone but the similarly-aged man grooving nearby. And rest assured, pool games don’t stop on Thursdays just because someone is playing guitar ten feet from the table.

Once you get sick of Innertown (and it happens after a while, the smallish confines and red-tinged atmosphere waxing claustrophobic over any number of hours and glasses of Pabst), a quick jaunt through the sleepy neighborhood will land you at Rainbo Club, on Damen just south of its intersection with Division.

The most prominent aspect of Rainbo Club is the round bar dominating half the room, billowing out in the center like the beer belly it aims to give you. Scowling bartenders with finely manicured mustaches and cool threads pull taps and pour shots, and God help you if you’re in the mood for a martini. You’ll need some luck to find a seat, since by the time you arrive at Rainbo Club (not a place to start your night but certainly a place to end it) seating will be at a premium. Maybe there will be a booth open somewhere along the far wall, or perhaps the sunken couch next to the Pacman machine will remain vacant, but there will certainly be no seats open at the bar. Where Innertown crafts an intimate vibe, Rainbo Club seems to want you up and about, talking, laughing, and joking with strangers.

It goes too far to call the place a meat market (Head over to the Crotch if that’s what you’re seeking), but if you are a guy who likes the idea of meeting a girl who wears brightly colored leggings beneath oversized flannel shirts, a girl who fancies meeting a guy who smells like a goat and looks like he’s in Band of Horses, or are any enemy of plastic whatsoever, then this is your place. With nothing much else to do (There’s the ignored Pacman arcade game, an ignored pinball machine, and a largely ignored photo booth), you either come with friends and reminisce about the past or come ready to make a new friend or two and maybe, just maybe, find the evening’s soul mate.

This process is blessedly inexpensive, three dollars for a pint of beer and four for a shot of the good stuff. The hum of conversation provides ample background noise, and smiling pretty kids who think they’ll live forever mingle gracefully with grizzled vets who know better. Rainbo Club is a welcoming place, where theatre majors discuss the news of the day with bikers turned philosophers. About the only things that would appear out of place at Rainbo Club would be starched collars or skinny heels, but patrons sporting these may very well find something to talk about with someone completely unlike them while standing in the open area between the back booths and the circular bar, dim lights muddying the scene as personal differences vanish amid the overwhelming din of conversation.

It can be annoying to stumble into a bar at which you cannot use your debit or credit card. Yet it can also be annoying to wait at a bar twenty minutes after last call to sign your tab, the lights up and revealing the formerly rocking club to be a room full of vacant-eyed strangers once so put-together but now mostly just washed-out. Credit cards breed amateurish drinking tendencies, with the whole of your bank account at hand and you able to order whatever comes to mind with continuously less inhibition. Throwing down on a round of shots sounds fine when resources are unlimited. And, almost always, this impulse is one that should be ignored.

By going the cash-only route, you’re setting yourself up for a specific kind of good time, one that’s on your own terms and during which there is a miniscule chance of some lush stumbling into you with a mouth full of vomit on his way to the bathroom. Before traveling to Gold Star, Innertown, or Rainbo Club, head to your bank and take out forty dollars. If everything goes right, you’ll still have fifteen or twenty bucks left in the morning, and perhaps a ripe stranger in bed next to you, a new friend with whom you can share an uncomfortable morning-after conversation while piecing together the previous night’s events.

And at least none of these is plastic.
