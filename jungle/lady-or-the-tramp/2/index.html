There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : Lady, or the Tramp, by Sally Weigel

Her vagina caught my attention especially since it’s so unnaturally kept. Presently, the prepubescent, hairless look is the norm, but it portrays a flawless image of beauty that isn’t real, complex, or mysterious. Again, Gaga’s vagina makes no statements about sex or the body. It just seems to advertise misogyny instead of the “strong and independent” womanhood she hopes to encourage. Aesthetically, Gaga appears no more exciting than one of those blow-up dolls that some men undiscerning substitute for real women. This begs the question, if parading her vagina is groundbreaking, what’s the point? Her fan base comprises a huge portion of homosexuals who presumably do not care to see her vagina. And if she is flaunting her body as a form of artistic expression, what is the meaning behind this move? Calling it a gesture of artistic expression does not make it so, especially with Gaga’s recent claim that she does not idolize and celebrate sex as much as her videos would suggest. It seems the celibate pop star gains her fame and sells her theatrics with the pornographic tone of her videos and Photoshopped spreads in magazines. Her fame has as much to do with her vagina as it has to do with her voice.

When I had first heard of Lady Gaga, I was excited to learn that she cited David Bowie as a major influence. My high-school years were spent listening to David Bowie’s “Hunky Dory” on repeat, and I was all the better for it. I learned early to break down the more base constrictions that accompany rigid gender roles. I learned how to shake my hips. Like Bowie, Gaga was frank about her bisexuality, and she represented a modern-day interpretation of glam rock. As she continued to disappoint, however, I became more and more angered that her popularity wholly eclipsed the most innovative, most theatrical, aesthetically bisexual performer of our day: Kevin Barnes from Of Montreal.

Before Gaga wrote, “Don’t want no money/ That shit’s ugly/ Just want your sex,” Barnes penned, “Can’t help it if it’s true/ Don’t wanna be your man/ Just wanna play with you.” As in the Gaga corpus, sex remains at the forefront of Barnes’s lyrics; but between the sexual rhetoric and primitive dance beats, he also manages to articulate complex feelings of depression, heartbreak, and nostalgia. Barnes’s personality celebrates androgyny, represents the absurdity of gender norms, and brings to light the body’s role in shaping one’s personhood. When he isn’t dressed in glitter and corsets, he wears more comfortable attire – colorful pants and a scarf, usually – but his femininity, even when subdued, is always apparent. His androgynous style isn’t compromised at any time. While flamboyant in his art, he is straight in his personal life, remaining committed to his wife and child. Barnes forces his audience to deal with the complexity of his persona and, in turn, realize the dense role sex plays in their own characters. While Gaga advertises her vagina, Barnes flaunts his body with an equal aversion to shame. He is known to dance on stage naked, sometimes masturbating in front of the crowd. For him, however, the production is neither beastly nor glamorized; it’s as if he just wants to see what happens when he lets his body control his mind.

Forget Madonna. In my adolescent years, Barnes and Bowie were my sexual icons. Their choices are unabashedly congruent with their characters, and so, as artists and public figures, they brave statements of the riskiest and most serious nature. While I’m not critical of Lady Gaga’s musings on abstinence or the ravenous promiscuity she simultaneously promotes in her videos, I’m painfully aware that, whatever ideology she promotes, it’s only ever done to sell herself. The sexual icon of the generation should be more than a prostitute.

    First of all, I am not offended by the mere mention of a critique about Lady GaGa- even as a very active fan. On the contrary, I have actually heard most of the arguments against her popularity. However, I have issues with calling her a hipocrite. The headline of this article only serves to perpetuate the stereotype that women can only fit into 2 categories: a lady, or a tramp. This idea is antiquated and really has no place in a world as sexually complex as today. If nothing else GaGa shows us that women CAN be somewhere in between. Other female pop artists have also held up this assumption: Britney started out a lady and then was transformed into a tramp, Christina was always a self-proclaimed tramp. There there were the pop stars that vocalized their “clean” images (think Jessica Simpson, Mandy Moore, and now Taylor Swift). Is it too hard to accept that a woman might be somewhere in between a lady and a whore? Additionally, the quotation about celibacy in this article was parsed. The actual declaration she made wasn’t simply “don’t have sex,” it was part of a longer speech. At an event for the Viva Glam foundation, GaGa stated: "I remember the cool girls when I was growing up. Everyone started to have sex. But it's not really cool any more to have sex all the time. It's cooler to be strong and independent. I can't believe I'm saying this - don't have sex. I'm single right now and I've chosen to be single because I don't have the time to get to know anybody. So it's OK not to have sex, it's OK to get to know people. I'm celibate, celibacy's fine. Something I do want to celebrate with my fans is that it's OK to be whomever it is that you want to be. You don't have to have sex to feel good about yourself, and, if you're not ready, don't do it." So instead of having an artist saying one thing and doing another, she is actually reaching out to her fans or those who may have been alienated by her strong sense of sexuality, which can often be misconstrued as being over sexual. She is the living example of the distinction between having a strong sexual presence and being sexually promiscuous.
