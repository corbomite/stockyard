“Mystery Tree”: A Critique

“The painting, on the other hand, cannot be comforted.”

by Brian R. Soucek | 06 May. 2009
Tawdry Details:
0 comments »
Volume I, Issue 1

To see more of Liz’s work, visit her website:
LizArtist.com.

Is it possible to be menaced by paisley?

The potential for aesthetic menace is clear to anyone who, like me, continues to deal with the repercussions of a decade (circa 1986-95) spent buying paisley ties. I’ve reduced these to a week’s worth – the metaphor of pruning seems apt in the current context – but they’ve now been joined by a paisley dress shirt, ottoman, and reading chair.

Few are more connected to their armchairs than we philosophers; still, the fact that Elizabeth Weber’s painting echoes even the colors of mine raises troubling questions about my principles of selection. Surely my interest in her work has no trace of the clichéd philistine preference for “paintings that match the couch.” Artworks, we know, are not mere instruments of design or elements of décor. The distinction is as ancient as Plato, who wrote what is to my mind not just the first philosophy of art, but also the first philosophy of couches, in Republic Book X. Yet even there Plato gives us the art/design distinction without endorsing the (to us) implied hierarchy; he infamously prefers the carpenter to the artist. And in this case the distinction is blurred by the work itself. Surely a painting that takes its central design element from a shawl won’t take offense at having been compared to a chair.

It shouldn’t, as it does particularly well by the comparison. The difference between a paisley painting and a paisley chair, Plato aside, is that the painting but not the chair can be about its paisleys. ‘‘Mystery Tree’’ brilliantly, emphatically, is about its paisleys. It completes the paisley’s odyssey of several centuries: the Persian vegetal motif brought to Europe by the East India Company, occasionally banned in France, mass produced and given its name in Scotland, imprinted on bandanas and painted on John Lennon’s Rolls Royce, is brought home in ‘‘Mystery Tree,’’ made again into leaves on a tree. Craftsmen turned nature into design in order to make shawls and scarves. Weber returned that design to nature in order to make art. Classical aesthetics often equivocated on whether art was the imitation of beautiful nature or the beautiful imitation of nature. In Weber’s stylized and stylish world the distinction ceases to matter: paisleys are artistically imposed on the very trees from which they were originally drawn. Weber’s painting depicts nature, designed. In doing so, it only makes explicit what has always been true: that artists are creationists.

Intelligent design, however, always suffers from the problem of evil. I haven’t, because I have so far ignored this work’s more troubling side: the obvious menace of the paisleys. It is clear even on first glance that the tree here is fending off an attack of paisleys more than it is sprouting them. The storm of associations prompted by their shape inevitably darkens: leaves, commas, protozoa, raindrops, tears. Are these paisleys raining on, or swimming through, the paint-streaked world of the painting? What are they consuming in their swarm? Have paisleys always harbored this aggression in their coils?

The meaning of the paisleys’ menace is beautifully, enigmatically, underdepicted. That’s not an apple tree, is it, which is being attacked by those—ahem—“Persian” motifs? I don’t know the artist, so I don’t know her fears: It could be Iran, or Afghanistan, or Kashmir; it could be – my reassurances above aside – a fear of consumer design encroaching on her art. Perhaps she finds it ominous that environmental destruction has become so modish to depict: a seasonal pattern which might any day become passé.

Ultimately, it’s pointless to ask what the artist fears. She is not here, and besides, her fears might always be assuaged. An election, a good review, a glass of wine—who knows what might cheer her up. The painting, on the other hand, cannot be comforted. Its fixedness as depiction stands in contrast to the fluidity of everything it depicts: the paisleys dropping like bombs, swarming around the tree; the streaks of paint in the background seeming still to drip. This fixedness is a “mere” ontological fact of painting, as of death. It is one of painting’s joys, even when – as here – what is fixed is fear. This particular painting’s sense of fear is as unshakeable as the object of its fear is unknowable. That is to say, fear is the wrong word; we are actually dealing here with dread. Having handled dread stylishly, as this painting no doubt does: that is hardly the worst of epitaphs.
