The Litterbug

All of the Ke$ha jabs just seem to express that a woman can be a whore in Hollywood as long as she does it with some class.

by Sally Weigel | 12 Aug. 2010
Tawdry Details:
1 comment »
Volume II, Issue 3

As she toys with her blonde, teased tresses, Ke$ha’s sun-kissed face and common features glare into the camera until her eyes roll off to the side in a drunken fog. Amid the knots of her long locks, one discerns an animal feather, a symbol of her bestial mores. Among the weighty assortment of metals and stones thrown on her ears, neck, and wrists, one spots an adornment in the form of an abused American flag twisted around her arm. She dons cut-off jean shorts that appear to be actually cut-off, wraps her arms around a haggard man who looks unemployed, and sports black mascara that seems to have aggregated over a full month of nights on the town with no pause for a nap or a shower. The following images mesmerize the viewer in Ke$ha’s “TiK ToK” music video, especially since the movie emulates and gloats in various stereotypes associated with “white trash.” Even though the critical responses have often been harsh, Ke$ha seems to be redefining glamour by finding inspiration in her Southern roots (which are not of the plantation kind).

While Ke$ha appears to be a product of at least a middle-class sensibility, she now glamorizes a life of morning-drinking (and afternoon- and night-drinking).

Before selling well over a million copies of her debut album, Ke$ha grew up in Nashville in the care of a mother who earned a living by writing songs for Dolly Parton, Johnny Cash, and other country acts. A formidable student, she earned a scholarship to study psychology and comparative religion at the prestigious Barnard College. While Ke$ha appears to be a product of at least a middle-class sensibility, she now glamorizes a life of morning-drinking (and afternoon- and night-drinking), flaunts garbage bags on her feet, and proclaims a preference for “fat, bearded” men. Yet this unwholesome image is nothing too groundbreaking for a pop singer. Gone are the days when the starlets did not sexualize their every career move, but so, too, have the days passed when their identities were not constructed by a team of stylists (Taylor Swift may very well be the only exception to both statements).

For most of the females landing on the Billboard Top Ten, their success is coupled with a Rachel Zoe in the background, who selects their clothes in the interest of packaging and selling their personae. Gaga is high-art, Beyonce is “fierce,” Rhianna is couture, and Ke$ha is trashy. Although all these brands may stem from the pop stars’ original personalities, it would be foolish to deflate how intentional and carefully constructed they are. For instance, the “white-trash” image of an unkempt Britney Spears walking barefoot around gas-station bathrooms seems a little too natural. Ke$ha takes it one step further, turning this image into high fashion. When she wore blue glittery eyeliner, there was most likely a conversation surrounding the choice. When she promenaded in cowboy boots in the videos for “TiK ToK” and “Your Love Is My Drug,” there might have been a hope for a future endorsement.  

The persona Ke$ha deploys has incited many reactions expressing distaste, many of which go further than just slamming her cheeky pop songs and malign her as a “dirty whore.” Yet she seems to have jumped the gun on her critics, nodding her head in agreement when they call her trashy. Although she continually sings about getting drunk (“E’erbody breaking bottles, it’s a filthy hot mess. / Gonna get faded, I’m not the designated driver, / So I don’t give a—”), so, too, does Gaga in her singles “Let’s Dance” and “Telephone.” Ke$ha’s other favorite topic seems to be the woes that accompany being blown-off by a guy (“Stephen / Why won’t you call me? / I’m sitting here waiting: / Why won’t you call me?”), which is comparable to almost any Taylor Swift song. Gaga and Swift’s lyrics may exhibit superior skill, but only slightly. Their voices are not subjected to auto-tune, either; but Rolling Stone’s critique of Ke$ha as “repulsive, obnoxious, and ridiculously catchy” seems to have gone too far. She is no more obnoxious than the Black Eyed Peas and no more repulsive than Fergie. All of the Ke$ha jabs just seem to express that a woman can be a whore in Hollywood as long as she does it with some class.
