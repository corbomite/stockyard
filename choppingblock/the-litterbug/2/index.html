There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : The Litterbug, by Sally Weigel
Photo Credit: PATDBR.com

Few mainstream female artists have so gladly adopted the persona of a dipsomaniacal slut. Courtney Love and Amy Winehouse come to mind, but neither was a pop star and the poor hygiene of each, of course, was less a product of stylistic deliberation than a result of drug addiction. As an “artist,” Ke$ha seems to be making a cultural statement about bringing the low into the high. Even more specifically, she seems to be reclaiming the “white-trash” aesthetic in a kind of class politics. If Ke$ha makes any cultural comment, it may well be that her image shows a disdain for the Victorian woman, the career woman, the Park Avenue woman. Flaunting leather skirts, torn T-shirts, and a head of hair that leaves little question as to what she was doing the night before, Ke$ha rejects the fully made-up, perfectly powdered tresses and hokey habiliments associated with Hollywood glam.

Street style has caught on: flannel, leather, beards, visible tattoos, and jean shorts are now popular on city blocks. During LA Fashion Week this spring, Richie Sambora and designer Nikki Lund debuted a collection called “White Trash Beautiful,” which sported looks that paired leather and animal prints with bold cuts and multitudes of sequins. High fashion and street culture have joined together to brand an identity no less constrained by economics, which is the amazing power of fashion: it can be inspired by the rural, lower class and somehow still manage to make its products accessible only to the urban, upper class. In Ke$ha’s case, it seems that her garbage-chic look rejects the strict aesthetic standards regulating those with money but does little to raise the level of respect for the Americans whose image is a symptom of economic disparity.

Regardless of the authenticity of Ke$ha’s aesthetic or the cultural comment it expresses, I find it immensely difficult to connect with the persona she has created. It can be unnerving to watch a twelve-year-old mouth the words of “Tik Tok” and pretend, after she has taken a swig from an imaginary bottle of Jack, to stumble out of the bathroom tipsy. These days, however, there’s an acknowledgment that a song is not to be taken literally (or seriously) if its goal is to make one dance. But what’s most unsettling to me is that Ke$ha’s public image is an uncanny representation of my own style.  I live for summer weekends, for breaking out my cut-off jeans shorts and waking up always a little bit blonder (and leaving many to wonder if I have lost my brush for one reason or another). I fully admit that I like my men with a little scruff, and I loathe the days I have to exchange my tousled apparel for work-appropriate attire.

Even though Ke$ha now claims to want to go country in the future (or at least evolve from the strictly biological pop songs of Animal), “TiK ToK” appears to have destined her for one-hit wonderdom. The single that made her famous, and the only redeemable piece on the album, is catchy and stylistically different from most songs on top-ten radio—but it was done by Uffie first (listen to Justice’s “The Party”). Her trashy image remains far more interesting than her music, and so, if she does stick around for a little while, I hope she doesn’t strive to “reinvent” herself. Instead I hope she continues to take advantage of the one occupation that does not force a comb to one’s hair. That luxury will evaporate when it’s time for her to find a career.
