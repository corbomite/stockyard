There's no place like home
( Page 2 of 2 ) : The Opposite of Transcendence, by J.P. Gorman
Like the article?

Join the Discussion!

At Passion Pit’s recent Chicago show, a group of friends carried one destroyed young man from the crowd at twenty after eight. No doubt vodka, pilfered from a parent’s liquor cabinet and drank too quickly from a plastic water bottle, played a role in the absentia. Later in the evening, an ambulance’s bright lights beat the headliner’s arrival by more than twenty minutes. Fights broke out. People smoked grass with brazen disregard for the strict No Smoking Indoors rule and were hoisted onto each other’s shoulders to attain a better view. Security personnel earned their keep dealing with the ruckus.

All of which sounds very punk rock, except this band has nothing to do with punk. Rather than rebellious, the hubbub was the utmost in conformity to social norms. A prepackaged, Meals-Ready-to-Eat flavor pervaded the scene. The Passion Pit crowd cared only about individual buzzes and bum-rushes, getting simultaneously as lit up and as close to the stage as possible by the time “their band” came on.

When the show commenced, a jolt of energy snapped through the assemblage. The lights dimmed, and thousands of people went absolutely ape shit, working themselves into an epic lather, and this for a band that played, for less than one hour, music displaying neither technical proficiency nor melodic ingenuity. It did sound familiar and comforting, a worthy soundtrack for happy, self-enveloped, Apple-approved bouncing.

Much like the majority of its fans, Passion Pit seems to mistake enthusiasm for skill and being energetic for being interesting. Almost every drum pattern features eighth notes on the high-hat with a snare on the downbeat, regardless of tempo. Fuzzy keyboards fill the air, but once the room is ensconced in cotton-candy noise, the sound stagnates. The crowd jumps and waves its arms to the beat, but the whole scene at a Passion Pit concert reeks of that tale told by an idiot—you know, the one signifying nothing. A dance party commences, but it is as passive as one can be, its leader wearing a sweater over a button-down Oxford and the light rig only blasting white or pink, and only every so often.

But then, a survey of the crowd reveals the truth: Passion Pit is what The Kids, capitalized and plural, are into. This band seems to have captured the imaginations (or lack thereof) of today’s youth in a way that few bands of any shape or size can currently claim. But without pharmaceutical enhancement or youthful enthusiasm, the scene surrounding this band is boring, and incredibly so. It seems incidental, and vastly out of proportion, to a band the majority of whose members stand behind keyboards and nod their heads while pretty pictures roll lazily and unnecessarily on LED screens behind them. The crowd moves in time to the beat, but no one is being moved.

Dancing is fun, as is getting hammered in public. Put the two together, and a good time is guaranteed: Passion Pit’s popularity proves as much. The door was open, and they walked through. For whatever reason, the current zeitgeist loves keyboards and falsetto atop disco drumbeats. Thanks to the assist from Apple, the zeitgeist found, and loves, Passion Pit. This band will be around, and popular, for a long time.

Which is a shame, because this band needed time to marinate and develop. Several thousand people jam Passion Pit’s live show, many of them still in high school and determined to have the time of their lives at their First Big Show, and very few of them seem to notice that nothing of much note happens, save brain-cell genocide and rampant fist pumps, pink lights flashing and keyboards blaring while a steady disco beat allows everyone to do exactly what they came to do with no worry of being surprised in any way, shape, or form.

It’s probably a lot of fun after six or seven Xanax and a couple cold beers. But under those conditions, so is everything else.
