There's no place like home
( Page 4 of 5 ) : Absinthe-Minded, by Timothy Hannifan

On a base of Kübler, a clear-colored absinthe blanche, the cocktail combines Ketel One vodka, lime, and ice water; the mixture is topped with grenadine, which slowly settles to the bottom of the glass. Lurking beneath the cocktail proper, the grenadine is impossible to taste in unison with the other ingredients, and the vodka adds more bite than flavor. So the dominant impression comes from the Kübler, which actually seems to louche as the cocktail is mixed. While it’s possible that the anise is even more subtle than in the Pernod, it’s likely that the water and vodka simply overwhelm its flavor; but, for whatever reason, only a vague, sweet licorice taste and slight fruitiness stand out. The finish is indistinct, although the numbed mouthfeel is noticeable. An uncomplicated drink, the coctakail is not as intriguing as The Violet Hour’s offerings, but it makes a perfectly fine use of absinthe. The drink’s one distinct flaw, a serious one, owes to the grenadine. I make a point of finishing all my drinks, just as I make a point of seeing every last call. Which is usually easy, but the puddle of grenadine at the bottom of my Absinthe Minded was hard to get down. I imagine it’s intended as a palate-cleanser – or, more correctly, -overwhelmer – for those who may not enjoy the taste of absinthe; but I would much prefer tasting even the worst cocktail (which the Absinthe Minded certainly is not) for an extra few moments to having my mouth coated in a sickly-sweet, pomegranate-flavored, high-fructose corn syrup for at least half a glass of water.

Although the lounge will also serve Kübler alone, minibar is neither mechanically equipped nor atmospherically inclined to realize the ritual. My bartender poured a shot of absinthe into a four-ounce glass, balanced a teaspoon and a cube of sugar above it, and sprinkled it with water. Since the absinthe didn’t fully louche, drinking it was like downing a shot of licorice-infused vodka. And it goes without saying that the aura of mystery and excitement that clings to the liquor fades almost to the point of vanishing without the trappings of the ritual, the loveliness of the louche, and the grandeur of the tools.

The Palmer House Hilton is a welcome presence in Chicago’s Loop. Down the street from the Marshall-Field’s-turned-Macy’s, surrounded by fast-food chains and seedy jewelry wholesalers, the hotel clings fast to a glamour that the area’s development has turned into a sad atavism. Its most stunning space is the lobby, a space so vaulted, gilded, opulent as to be just this side of Technicolor.

Potter’s Lounge is a smaller – the appropriate review jargon is “more intimate,” I believe, but Potter’s isn’t quite that – bar in the Palmer House. For the most part, its dim lighting and dark color scheme create an understatedly upscale atmosphere; and the lounge is comfortable and unpretentious while generally maintaining a patina of class. The design trips over two elements, both allusions to the bar’s former life as a jazz lounge, one less forgivable than the other. One wall of the central floor space is covered in black-and-white photos of performers who appeared in the old lounge; plastic photos that fluoresce painfully bright, they give any poor reviewer who unwittingly stares at them too long fantasies of floating mechanically, inexorably down a contaminated, ersatz stream running through Disney’s It’s a Small Lounge After All. The available seating rarely compels the sitter to focus on this particular wall, however, and so those without a morbid Nietzschean compulsion to abyss-gaze will find it mercifully easy to look away. If this particular aesthetic flaw presents as benign but is unforgivable for reasons that are not, the space’s other shortcoming is offensive for obvious reasons but is ultimately charming. A short flight of steps leads from the main seating area to the bar proper; on either side of these steps, encased in glass and hemmed in by small floodlights, is a pillar coated in white feathers. Ugly as these pillars are, they turn out to be there – a manager informed me when, after a few absinthes, it seemed appropriate to ask what these two upstanding structural supports had done to warrant tarring and feathering – because the Potter Palmer for whom lounge and hotel are named was a notorious fan-dancing fan, and he often invited dancers to perform in the space that is now Potter’s Lounge. The pillars turn out to be delightfully kitschy, the photos discomfitingly false: both deal with the hotel’s golden age, but the pillars are meant as gaudily subversive tongue-in-cheek references to the Palmer House’s history, whereas the photos are meant seriously to turn Potter’s into a simulacrum of its past.

This tension – between charming reference and unsettling falsity – turns out to run deeper than the décor. And so the bartender serves absinthe in near-perfect accordance with the grandest variety of the ritual, but serves an actual liquor that tastes vaguely artificial, slightly but detectably off. Le Tourment Vert was difficult to see properly in the dim light, but it appeared a blue-green vaguely reminiscent of hypo-chlorinated pool water. Which was a bad initial sign: Absinthe should look natural, not chemical. I was distracted from the off-putting color, however, when the bartender produced a beautiful absinthe spoon – silver, shaped like a leaf by way of Quentin Crisp, flourishing melodramatically – and a gigantic fountain: a crystal basin of ice water with four spigots protruding from the bottom, supported by a pewter Art Nouveau woman. The stunning setup drew the attention of the entire bar.
